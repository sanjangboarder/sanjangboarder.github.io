While the webfoot octopus is often cited as the most popular target among cephalopods, the larger stone octopus (*common octopus*) maintains a dedicated following. Edible octopuses in Korea are primarily split into two species: the giant Pacific octopus (*dae-mun-eo*) and the stone octopus (*cham-mun-eo*). The massive specimens shown on TV are giant Pacific octopuses, while the smaller varieties found in local markets are stone octopuses.

​

Stone octopuses are primarily targeted along the West and South coasts. Closed season (fishing ban) dates vary by region, split into three zones: the West Coast (national), the South Coast, and Jeju Island. Understanding these dates is critical for planning your trips, which I detail below.

​

---

​

When fishing, two rules are critical: first is safety, and second is observing closed seasons. Observing closed seasons is our way of showing respect to the ocean. Protecting spawning females ensures robust stocks for future seasons.

### 2026 Regional Closed Seasons for Stone Octopus

I compiled the chart below to outline the open and closed dates:

​

The West Coast and general national waters open for fishing on **July 1**. This applies nationwide, excluding Jeonnam, Gyeongnam, and Jeju. Since the West Coast represents the primary fishery outside those areas, you can treat this as the West Coast guideline.

​

The South Coast opens on **July 9**, and Jeju Island opens on **September 16**, reflecting its local water temperature cycles. The opening day in each region marks the start of the primary fishing season.

Violating closed seasons carries severe penalties, including fines up to 20 million KRW or up to two years in prison. For stone octopus (*common octopus*), there is no official minimum landing size. However, releasing small specimens (under the size of a webfoot octopus) is recommended to preserve resources. For giant Pacific octopus, harvesting specimens under 600g is strictly prohibited.

### Octopus Lifecycles: A Humble Spawning Journey

The standard name for what we call "stone octopus" (*dol-mun-eo*) is the **common octopus** (*cham-mun-eo*). Before they become targets for sport fishing, these intelligent creatures lead a complex lifecycle. An octopus's life ends shortly after reproduction. Males die within months of mating, while females dedicate their remaining energy to protecting their eggs. For months after spawning, the female protects the eggs without feeding.

​

> **"She passes away only after the eggs hatch safely."**

​

This maternal dedication completes the cycle of life in the ocean. Observing closed seasons is not simply a legal requirement; it is a sign of respect for this spawning process.

### Why do Closed Seasons Vary by Region?

Varying regional dates reflect natural spawning timelines, which depend on local water temperatures. Understanding these regional differences is basic knowledge for cephalopod anglers.

​

*   **General / National (Incheon, Gunsan, Ulsan, Busan, etc.):** May 16 – June 30 *(Opens July 1)*
*   **Jeonnam & Gyeongnam:** May 24 – July 8 *(Opens July 9)*
*   **Jeju Island:** August 1 – September 15 *(Opens September 16)*

​

Additionally, avoid confusing these dates with long-arm octopus (*nakji*) closed seasons. Long-arm octopus closures differ, running through June in Chungnam and from June 16 to July 31 in Gyeongnam. Double-check local dates for your specific location before departing.

### Understanding "Intent to Harvest"

Anglers sometimes assume that catching and immediately releasing a fish during a closure is acceptable. However, maritime law evaluates the "intent to harvest." Rigging dedicated multi-jig octopus rigs during a closed season can be interpreted as intent to harvest, regardless of whether you release the catch.

​

*   **Common / Stone Octopus:** No legal minimum landing size, but releasing small individuals is recommended.
*   **Giant Pacific Octopus:** Harvesting specimens under 600g is strictly prohibited.

​

### The Opening Day Golden Window

When the closed season opens, catch rates peak. Octopuses are territorial, and the populations that accumulated during the closure are heavily caught during the first three days.

​

</div>

​

*   **West Coast (Chungnam, Gunsan, etc.):** Opens July 1 at 00:00. Fishing starts on the morning of July 1. Peak cycles occur every 3 to 4 years. Having peaked in 2024, 2026 is less likely to see record catches, but remains worth targeting.
*   **South Coast (Jeonnam, Gyeongnam):** Opens July 9 at 00:00. Anglers typically board boats on the evening of July 8 and wait on the water for midnight. Outside of heavy rainfall years, opening day consistently yields excellent catches. South Coast charter fleets run double sessions (day and night), making it the prime time to fish.

​

Catch rates decline quickly after the first two to three days. Unlike webfoot octopus, stone octopus populations are limited, and pressure reduces numbers quickly. Booking a weekday slot on opening day is highly recommended.

​
