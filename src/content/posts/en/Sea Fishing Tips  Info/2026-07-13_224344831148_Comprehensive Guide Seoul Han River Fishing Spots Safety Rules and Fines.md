Have you ever spotted people fishing along the Han River? While it is easy to assume that fishing is banned along the entire stretch of the river, only about 53% of the Han River is designated as a no-fishing zone. As long as you strictly follow the regulations within the permitted zones, you can find excellent spots just a 5-minute walk from nearby parking. Catching fish right in the middle of Seoul is a unique and exciting hobby!

​

Seoul officially permits fishing in surprisingly many areas. In general, fishing is strictly prohibited directly under or near all bridges. Popular parks where pedestrian paths and bicycle lanes sit close to the water, such as Yeouido Han River Park, Seonyudo Island, and Nodeulseom Island, are completely restricted. Additionally, the areas north of Jamsil are protected water source zones, meaning they are also entirely banned. Aside from these areas, several excellent spots permit fishing.

Now that we've covered the restricted zones, let me recommend the best spots in the permitted areas.

​

Han River fishing spots are generally divided into **Official Fishing Platforms** and **Wild Spots**. Official platforms are flat, safe, and easy to set up rod holders, making them ideal for targeting freshwater species like carp, mullet, and eels using float rigs. On the other hand, wild spots are stone-embanked embankments with less foot traffic, requiring some walking. These spots are perfect for lure fishing, targeting aggressive predators like predatory carp (skyager), mandarin fish, and largemouth bass.

​

For the Seoraeseom spot, timing is key. Tide times at Incheon/Ganghwa impact this area roughly 3 hours later. At low tide, the water level drops significantly, requiring you to move further out to the main current. The best window is from high tide to +6 hours. High tide here peaks roughly 3 hours after Incheon's high tide, so plan your trip around that window.

The species you can target in the Han River vary. For lure fishing, skyagers, mandarin fish, and largemouth bass are the main targets. In the main currents, targetting skyagers is best, though seabass or mullet will occasionally strike. In backwaters and slower currents, you can expect mandarin fish or bass, so matching your rigs to the target species is essential.

Here are critical rules for Han River fishing that you must follow to protect the environment and stay safe:

​

<div class="single-image">
<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA3MTJfMTg3/MDAxNzgzODYyODM4OTUw.pAcstSIY87zyIRNJh42U8AafoUnizDHVuvtOG-MMvN8g.70yYRFYxAC67YANUAT7r8Co5Def6z4gaa-7IwT3rXrkg.PNG/image.png?type=w800" />
</div>

*   **🚫 No Entry in Restricted Zones**: Fishing is strictly banned under or near bridges, along high-traffic bicycle paths, and around boat docks. Always look for no-fishing signs.
*   **🎣 Limit of 3 Rods**: Using 4 or more fishing rods per person is a violation of the city ordinance.
*   **🐟 No Pollution-Causing Baits**: To protect water quality, dough/powder baits (tteokbap) are banned. You must use artificial lures or live worms.
*   **❌ No Illegal Fishing Practices**: Snagging (snatching), throw nets, harvesting shellfish, and catching sweetfish (eun-eo) are strictly banned. Fishing from watercraft (boats) is also prohibited.
*   **🚨 Follow Evacuation Orders**: In dangerous situations, such as increased discharge from Paldang Dam, you must follow official evacuation orders immediately.

​

Violating these rules carries hefty fines, so please play by the rules:

​

Finally, a few quick tips for Han River fishing:

The main lure targets are skyagers (predatory carp), mandarin fish, barbel steed (nuchi), and Japanese lates (seabass). Skyagers are the most common catch, while bass are harder to target due to fast currents. Because the river flows into the sea, it experiences tidal changes. Adding 2 to 4 hours to Incheon/Ganghwa tide schedules will give you the high tide time for the Han River, which will boost your catches.
