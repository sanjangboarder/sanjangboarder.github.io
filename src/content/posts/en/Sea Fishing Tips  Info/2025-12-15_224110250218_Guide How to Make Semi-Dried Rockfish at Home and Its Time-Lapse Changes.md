Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

One of my minor goals this winter is to make semi-dried rockfish (*Korean rockfish / 우럭*) at home. Since it is currently the peak of the rockfish season at Seonjae Fishing Park on Seonjae Island, which I visit frequently, I expect to catch plenty of rockfish through next spring.

​

I usually bring back four to five rockfish per outing. While some are eaten fresh as sashimi, I have the remainder prepared using a butterfly cut (*back-slit butchering / 등따기*) at the local cleaning station. Butterflied rockfish are suitable for spicy fish soup (*Maeuntang*) or grilling, but semi-drying them significantly improves their flavor when prepared as salted rockfish soup (*Ureok-jeotguk / 우럭 젓국*), steamed rockfish, or grilled fish.

​

The required items for semi-drying are straightforward, though the process demands some effort. You need salt, a measuring cup, and a container large enough to submerge the fish in brine. This brine bath extracts moisture and seasons the flesh. Finally, you require a hanging line or a fish drying net.

For this batch, I processed four rockfish. I placed them in the tub first to estimate the volume of brine required. The fish weighed between 800g and 1kg when caught, which is a key factor when considering brine concentration and soaking times.

​

​

I estimated that 4 to 5 liters of brine would be necessary. In previous attempts, I dry-salted the fish by sprinkling salt directly onto the flesh, which is effective but can lead to uneven seasoning. To ensure consistent salinity, I opted for a wet brine bath.

​

​

My recipe uses **two paper cups of salt for every 3 liters of water**. This yields roughly a 12% salinity level. Since standard seawater is around 3.5% to 4% salinity, this brine is significantly saltier. I measured 4.5 liters of water and added three cups of salt (using pink salt for this batch). Adding a splash of Soju and vinegar helps control fish odors, though this is optional.

​

For fresh, high-quality catches, these odor-control additions can be omitted.

​

​

To keep the fish fully submerged despite buoyancy, I placed a weighted container on top.

​

I have run this brining process twice this winter. In my first attempt, I soaked the fish for two hours. The seasoning was generally correct, though the thickest sections of the flesh remained slightly mild. For the second run, I extended the soak to three hours, which resulted in a more consistent, slightly saltier profile.

​

​

After brining for two to three hours, I prepared the fish for drying. I removed them from the brine, rinsed them under cold tap water, and patted them dry. I hung the drying net in my air conditioner outdoor unit utility room. To manage any dripping liquid and prevent odors, I lined the bottom shelf of the drying net with plastic sheeting.

​

I mounted a rod in the utility room and suspended the net to ensure airflow. My apartment in Songdo, Incheon, overlooks the sea, allowing me to utilize the natural sea breeze for drying.

Two weeks of drying produced a suitable texture for steaming. For a firmer, chewier texture, extending the drying period to three weeks is viable. Drying the fish for three to four weeks to remove deeper moisture would allow you to shred the flesh and mix it with gochujang paste, replicating the style of barley-aged yellow croaker gochujang (*Bori-gulbi gochujang*).
