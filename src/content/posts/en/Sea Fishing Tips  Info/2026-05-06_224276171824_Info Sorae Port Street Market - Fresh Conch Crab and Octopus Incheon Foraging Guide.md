Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

I regularly go shallow-water foraging (haerujil) around Incheon. For shore-walking foraging, you can target various spots like Incheon Songdo, Muuido Island, Yeongjongdo Island, Yeongheundo Island, and the Sihwa Seawall. However, it requires carefully matching the tides, setting up heavy gear, and catches of target species like conch (sora), whelk (golbaengi), mantis shrimp (gaetgajae), or octopus (nakji) are never guaranteed.

​

During my Incheon foraging trips, I have harvested plenty of conch and whelk, and picked up octopus and blue crab. May represents the peak season when conch, mantis shrimp, whelks, crabs, and octopus are highly active and plentiful.

​

However, not everyone has the time or gear to go foraging. If you are seeking efficiency in terms of cost and effort, purchasing them at a fish market is a highly realistic, superior alternative. In this post, I introduce the **Sorae Port Open-Air Market (Nanjeon)**, where you can purchase fresh seafood harvested directly by local fishermen at Incheon foraging spots.

​

Parking at the public lot and walking past the Sorae History Museum leads you to the Sorae Port markets.

​

​

Incheon Sorae Port carries a notorious reputation for overcharging tourists, which kept me away for years. However, the open-air market (Nanjeon) located behind the Traditional Fish Market offers seafood at highly reasonable prices. In the map below, **A** indicates the Sorae Traditional Fish Market, and **B** indicates the open-air street market (Nanjeon). You can also enter through the Suhyup (Fisheries Cooperative) auction hall.

​

Walking through the Sorae Port Traditional Fish Market to reach the open-air stalls is the most convenient route and offers plenty of sights.

A short walk leads to the Sorae Port Traditional Fish Market. Sorae Port features three distinct markets: the Traditional Fish Market, the General Fish Market, and the Open-Air Market (Nanjeon). The General Fish Market is the one infamous for overcharging tourists, whereas the Traditional Fish Market is reasonably fair.

​

I headed to the cheapest option: the open-air Nanjeon stalls, where fishermen bring in their catch directly.

​

The open-air market starts at the far end of the Traditional Fish Market. I recommend checking prices at the traditional market as you walk through.

Here, you can find a diverse range of seafood harvested from the Incheon coast. While most are fresh-chilled catches, live fish are also available.

A wide selection of seasonal fish is displayed. The large basket of flathead (jang-dae) below cost just 10,000 KRW. This is remarkably cheap. While flatheads are often ignored by boat anglers, they taste fantastic when semi-dried and steamed. Here, giant specimens are available for pennies.

They also sell fresh-chilled monkfish, flatfish, and butterfish at exceptionally low prices.

Shrimp are also plentiful. Buying shrimp now makes May salted shrimp (o-jeot), while waiting until June yields June salted shrimp (yuk-jeot). A large tub costs roughly 30,000 to 40,000 KRW. The sellers mix it with coarse salt on the spot, so you can place it directly in your kimchi refrigerator at home to cure.

Large piles of mantis shrimp (gaetgajae) and ghost shrimp (ssok)—common Incheon foraging targets—are available. A basket of these costs around 10,000 KRW per kilogram.

​

They are exceptionally fresh. Catching a full kilogram of these when foraging requires major effort, but a single 10,000 KRW note buys a generous portion.

I purchased three live octopuses—a species that is rare to find when foraging. They were medium-sized, but plenty large for sashimi (tangtangi), and cost 20,000 KRW. The seller cleaned the internals and packed them. Even after driving home and stopping for coffee—arriving home 2 hours later—they were still highly active and fresh.

​

Spending 50,000 KRW at the Sorae Port open-air market fed our family of four to our limits with conch and octopus.

​

While I have gone foraging around Incheon many times, I have rarely brought home enough to satisfy the entire family. This purchase represented 50,000 KRW of pure happiness.

​

Tidal ranges in Incheon are large and currents are fast, making solo foraging hazardous. Foraging catches are also highly inconsistent, and cleaning mud-packed shells is tedious. I recommend doing foraging primarily for exercise and fun, and visiting the Sorae Port open-air market to purchase your seafood at a low price.
