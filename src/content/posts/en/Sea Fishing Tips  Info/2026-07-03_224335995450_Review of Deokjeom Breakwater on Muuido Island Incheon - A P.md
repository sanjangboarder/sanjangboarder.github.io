Hello, this is Mountain Cabin Border.

Last time, I explained about the well-known Muuido Serengeti point in Incheon, where you can expect large Spanish mackerel during the season, as well as flounder, hairtail, and octopus. Muuido Serengeti, or Mu-rangeti, is honestly not an easy place to access. Those who are accustomed to backpacking, running, or hiking might find it relatively easy, but those with even slightly uncomfortable legs or who are not physically prepared could definitely find it challenging.

Besides Mu-rangeti, Muuido also has easily accessible points that don't require mountain climbing. These are the Deokjeom Breakwater and the surrounding rock fishing points in Muuido. I'd like to tell you more about these places.

The image below shows the Deokjeom Breakwater to the rock fishing points. The numbers indicating distances below represent the distance from where the water comes in at high tide to where there's still depth even at the lowest point of low tide during spring tides. I will explain each point in detail below.

First, let's look at Muuido Deokjeom Breakwater. On the map, it's only a few minutes' walk from Gwangmyeong Port, and parking is available around the Deokjeom Breakwater, making its accessibility perhaps the best. However, it's very popular these days, so even if you go early on a weekend morning, parking nearby is often difficult, and you frequently have to use a public parking lot.

The Deokjeom Breakwater marks the distance from the completely dry land section, where water doesn't reach even at high tide, to the area where water remains even at low tide during spring tides. You'll find that if you go past the middle of the breakwater, it's not difficult to cast your lure to where the water is, even at high tide.

However, even if there's water at low tide, the depth isn't enough for fish to move freely or come close to the shore. To target bottom-dwelling fish like flounder, hairtail, octopus, or cuttlefish, you'll need a casting distance of at least 30 meters at high tide to reach a point where you can effectively appeal to them.

So, if you assume a casting distance of about 50m, you can retrieve about 20m while enticing fish. What I've just described is for high tide. However, at low tide, a lot of the seabed is exposed, as shown in the aerial map, which means you can target the hot spots even with 20-30m less casting distance compared to high tide. In short, Deokjeom Breakwater is easier to fish at low tide.

The image below shows Deokjeom Breakwater at low tide. There's a short, flat stone embankment at the bottom of the breakwater, which becomes exposed at low tide. It's always exposed around spring tides, but there are days when it's not exposed around neap tides.

In front of the stone embankment, the depth doesn't drop sharply like a cliff; rather, it seems there's a submerged ledge. This means if you cast long and then retrieve while keeping your lure near the bottom, or if you don't retrieve quickly when it's near your feet, you'll almost certainly get snagged on this ledge.

Beginners might find snagging to be a big problem here. A tip I can give you is to use fishing line with different colors every 10 meters, like a multi-colored braided line, which can help you remember the snagging locations. If you always see the same color line when you get snagged, you can retrieve quickly once that specific color appears during your next cast.

Also, one characteristic of the Deokjeom Breakwater point is that it's a channel. Therefore, the current is quite strong, and your line will drift significantly left or right with the incoming or outgoing tide. If you add wind to that, your rig might be retrieved from the right or left, even if you cast straight forward. If you're fishing alone, it's not a big problem, but if there are many people, it's best to pay attention to where others' lines are drifting and be considerate.

In lure fishing, letting your line drift endlessly while you keep hitting the bottom, or in float fishing, letting your float drift to where it interferes with others, or in surf fishing, letting a light sinker continuously roll – these are all considered inconsiderate. This spot is where lure fishing, float fishing, and surf fishing coexist, so it's a place where considerate fishing is needed, along with greeting fellow anglers.

The species I've caught here include rockfish, wrasse, flounder, Spanish mackerel, hairtail, gizzard shad, and octopus. In fact, you could say all fish species found in the Incheon area appear here, making it an excellent point, but getting a bite is by no means easy. Different methods can be used to target specific species depending on the tide, high/low tide, and current strength, and there are advantageous times, so I think it's a great spot with the fun of researching and strategizing your approach.

Next up are the Deokjeom Breakwater rock fishing points. Accessibility here is also incredibly good, but you can't enter at high tide. You can only get past the rock formations when the tide has receded somewhat, around mid-outgoing tide, as shown in the image below. You also need to leave before mid-incoming tide.

There is also a path that descends along the mountain trail leading to Mu-rangeti, which you can use to enter around high tide, but it's physically more demanding, so it's an option.

As seen on satellite maps or nautical charts, most areas have a distance of over 40m from the point where water doesn't reach at high tide to the point where it recedes maximally at low tide. This means if you cast 50m at high tide and retrieve, you'll immediately encounter a rocky bottom with severe snagging. Therefore, for bottom-dwelling fish at high tide, you'd likely need a casting distance of around 100m to make it worthwhile. It's more accurate to consider this a point for fishing around low tide.

After entering the rock formations, it looks like a beach, but it's actually a field of small rocks. If you target the bottom with metal lures, you'll experience a nightmare of snags. However, from where the water doesn't recede at low tide, the rocky field largely ends, so targeting the area beyond that is key.

A little past the beach, there's a protruding area like a cape... you can consider this the main point.ㅁㅋ

The fish species found here are almost the same as at Deokjeom Breakwater. The differences are that it requires longer casting distances, the water is a bit shallower, and it's less affected by the current. This makes it slightly more advantageous for targeting flounder, hairtail, etc.

And because it's wider, it can accommodate more people. Since the rocky field extends broadly, it would be correct to say that fishing around high tide, except for migratory species like Spanish mackerel, hairtail, or mackerel, is largely pointless.

Incheon Muuido has many more fishing points besides Mu-rangeti and Deokjeom Breakwater, which I've shown you. I think it might be one of the easier places for beginner anglers to start, with a relatively low difficulty level.

However, catching fish is by no means easy here. A tip I can give you is that going during the lowest point of low tide is particularly necessary for beginners, as it reduces casting distance limitations. If you start catching flounder or Spanish mackerel here, you'll have developed the basic skills to fish effectively anywhere in Muuido, so I think consistently going and trying your luck will yield good results.
