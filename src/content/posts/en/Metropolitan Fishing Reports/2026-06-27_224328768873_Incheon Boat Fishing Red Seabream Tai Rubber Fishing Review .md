Hello, this is Sanjangboder.

Originally, a boat fishing trip for tai rubber in Incheon was booked for last week, but unfortunately, it was canceled due to wind and rain. I was really disappointed because I wanted to test out my new Aomax tai rubber rod. So, I took a day off during the week and went to try my luck at catching sea bream, choosing one of the boat fishing charter companies at Incheon's Yeonam Port.

I've been to this charter company once last year; it's the Wangmanduho, part of the Khan Fishing fleet. I stopped by the office to sign the roster, and somehow I wasn't entirely confident I could entice the fish with just tai rubber skirts. So, I also paid 10,000 won for medium-sized worms before heading off to board the Wangmanduho.

Yeonam Port is the most popular harbor for boat fishing in Incheon. We boarded from Yeonam Pier 1. I thought there would be fewer people on a weekday, but haha, it wasn't much different from a weekend. Recently, some charter companies in the Incheon area struggle to gather enough customers on weekdays and can't depart, and it gets even worse when you go down to the Chungnam area. But Yeonam Port in Incheon, being a top-tier location, is still popular even on weekdays.

Perhaps it's a natural result, given its excellent accessibility and the short travel time back after fishing. Since I live in Songdo, Incheon, I took a taxi without a car that day. It cost 11,000 won... pretty similar to parking fees, haha.

I took the Wangmanduho once last year for cuttlefish and octopus, and I remember the boat's condition being excellent. Honestly, there are only a handful of sea bream tai rubber charter companies in the Incheon area. There are a few around Yeongheungdo, and at Yeonam Port, Rookie No. 2, Maruho, and Wangmanduho are probably all there are. In fact, there isn't much choice. My reason for choosing Wangmanduho was simple: I thought it might be more comfortable due to having plenty of empty spots.

It's over 50km from Yeonam Port. And the depth... The photos below show depths of 38-39m, but adding the tide, it means we were fishing all day for sea bream tai rubber in deep spots exceeding 40m.

The weather could be considered quite good. It was the 3rd tide, when the water starts to become more active, with a moderate breeze. The sky was clear, but the gentle wind kept it from being too hot, and the current was just right. From my perspective, these seemed like optimal conditions.

I remember usually doing tai rubber fishing in the Incheon area around Yeongheungdo or Jawoldo. Curious why we came so far, I asked the chief mate. He said that the water temperature near Incheon (Jawoldo) has risen considerably, and in such cases, they sometimes move to spots near Daesan, where the water temperature is lower, which is why we came so far. When I checked the water temperatures, it was indeed a bit lower around Daesan (towards Dangjin/Seosan) than in the Incheon area.

I set up two rods. I paired the Aomax Navigator tai rubber rod with a Creman Giant Little reel. I set the shock leader to size #4 in case a big one bit, and prepared NH Korea's head and a pink Boom Boom skirt. I started with a 60g head, but since we were always fishing in depths over 40m, I eventually went up to 100g.

There were no bites. Haha. I even started doubting if sea bream actually existed in this sea, as I couldn't even see a glimpse of one from the entire boat's catch report. Haha. And the situation was quite boring... For tai rubber, I usually reel 5 to 15 turns. Depending on the conditions, if it's near the bottom, I do a short retrieve, and if I'm unsure, I repeatedly reel up and down up to 15 turns.

While doing 'live tai rubber fishing,' I remembered the fresh lugworms I bought, so I put one on. Suddenly, a strong bite from the bottom... I reeled it in, and it was a croaker! Haha, I was a bit surprised. It's not well-expressed in the photo, but it was a really big croaker, well over 30cm. This is the size I usually see on deep-sea croaker trips to Mageomto, but these guys are already showing up here...

Given the nature of tai rubber fishing, where you repeatedly drop and retrieve, it gets very boring when nothing bites. Around the 15th turn, I felt a 'todok~' then 'totototodok jiiik~'—a bite that took the line, and I thought, 'This must be a sea bream!' It was quite heavy and put up a strong fight, haha. But when I reeled it in, it was a rockfish, lol. It was well over 40cm. Perhaps the rockfish was moving, or it bit at a higher depth, not near the bottom.

What I was most curious about that day was the Aomax Navigator rod. From my perspective, it's a rod that incorporates everything you'd expect from a high-end tai rubber rod, offering that perfectly 'chewy' (responsive/flexible yet strong) feel. The Hue's T700, with its Toray T700 solid blank, was also satisfying, but the Navigator, using a higher-grade blank material, felt even more robust and sharp, which was impressive.

I was a bit concerned because it's so slender, but there was no problem even when lifting rockfish or large longdae straight up. Although the sea bream size was disappointing, controlling this rod felt effortless. I think I'll need to hook a large sea bream to truly appreciate its full value, so I'll probably have to go on another sea bream trip just to test this rod. If anyone was curious about this rod, I don't think you'll regret giving it a try.

Although it ended with a disappointing catch, I had scheduled to rest for a day and then participate in the Aomax Downshot Tournament at Daecheon Port the next day. So, in my next fishing report, I'll share my experience from the tournament.
