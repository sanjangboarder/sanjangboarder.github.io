Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

During the winter season, Seonjae Fishing Park's Lure Zone closed for renovations. Last Saturday, the original basin reopened, partitioned into two sections: a **Mini Float Pond** and a **Mini Lure Pond**.

​

The park accepted opening day reservations for up to 80 lure anglers and 80 float anglers. While the Lure Pond reached full capacity, the Mini Float Pond drew approximately 40 anglers. Because I enjoy both lure casting and commercial float fishing, I booked a slot at the less crowded Mini Float Pond.

​

Fishing began at 7:30 AM on opening day. I arrived 30 minutes early, finding the main office crowded. Although the management had prepared, the check-in process was busy. We confirmed our bookings and entered the gates at 7:30 AM.

Before sharing the session details, I will cover the updates to the Mini Float Pond rules.

​

The previous lure entry fee of 120,000 KRW for 10 hours was considered high by some anglers. With the reopening, the hourly cost has been reduced by approximately 30% to **100,000 KRW for 12 hours**.

​

​

The availability of short-session tickets on weekends is a positive update. Because a 12-hour session is physically demanding, I expect to use the 6-hour short-session ticket, priced at 70,000 KRW (*slightly over 11,000 KRW per hour*), which represents a reasonable option.

​

Stocking frequency has been reduced by one session to twice daily. However, the volume of fish released per session remains high, consistent with Seonjae's standard.

​

The range of permitted live baits is broad, including squid strips and baby octopus. While commercial float ponds typically restrict movement, this zone allows anglers to move around the basin. This configuration differs from other venues like Manjeong Mint, which allow changing positions but follow a different layout.

​

The key operational difference between the Lure Pond and the Mini Float Pond lies in line regulations. Lure casting typically utilizes braided lines, but **braided lines are prohibited in the Mini Float Pond**. Because I have experience in float fishing, I used reels spooled with semi-floating monofilament lines. Anglers transitioning from the Lure Pond may find this rule restrictive.

​

The prohibition of braided lines is intended to minimize wind-drift and line tangles. Given the short casting distances and moderate angler density on opening day, braided lines would have been manageable. I expect the facility may reconsider this rule in the future.

​

Here is my tackle configuration. I utilized my existing Lure Zone rod while adjusting the remaining tackle components.

​

For float rigs, I used a high-visibility stick float: the **Haedong Master Long-Cast No. 1 Float**, which offers good casting weight. I will compile a dedicated setup guide for Seonjae's float pond in a future post, as the opening session clarified the ideal rig parameters.

During the first three hours, I landed eight rockfish. The holdover fish from last season were long but lean, whereas the newly stocked fish were dark and thick. The morning catch was split evenly between holdover and fresh fish.

​

The bites were subtle, requiring close observation of the float to identify bait takes. The decisive float submergences were associated with freshly stocked fish, suggesting that the holdovers had either moved toward the Lure Pond or remained inactive.

Activity slowed after 11:00 AM, so we took an early lunch break before starting the afternoon session. I surveyed the reef structures, but did not receive bites, suggesting the environmental variables were not aligned.

​

For the afternoon, I adjusted my tactics. The central bridge area is outside the direct current of the waterwheel, leaving the float stationary or subject only to wind drift. The bridge pilings did not produce fish, and targeting the cage net walls was also unproductive.

The productive pattern involved utilizing the waterwheel current, which pushes water rapidly toward the entrance. I cast my rig to drift along the riprap slope.

​

The basin features a breakline depth of approximately 3 meters, with the deepest channels reaching 3.5 to 4 meters. I set my float depth to 2.5 meters and drifted the rig along the slope, which produced three rockfish. I landed one additional fish from the morning reef spot around 2:00 PM.

A review of the basin showed that a few anglers reached double-digit figures, while beginners landed between 3 and 5 fish.

During the remaining time, I left my rod in the holder. The facility has installed rod holder receptacles along the banks of both the float and lure ponds. Anglers using float rigs will find bringing a rod stand convenient. During the afternoon stocking, I estimated approximately 80 fish were released, with about 10 landed by the time I left at 5:00 PM. This indicates a portion of the stocked fish remained in the basin, which should improve catch rates on subsequent sessions when weather variables align.

​

I also surveyed the Lure Pond, where catches appeared higher than in the Float Pond. This may have been caused by late piling construction for the floating cages in the Float Zone, which likely pushed fish toward the Lure side. Because net barriers now prevent migration between the basins, checking recent catch reports for both ponds should help guide choice of zone on future trips.

​

I plan to fish both the Lure Pond and the Mini Float Pond this season, and will share detailed reports on holding spots and rigs.
