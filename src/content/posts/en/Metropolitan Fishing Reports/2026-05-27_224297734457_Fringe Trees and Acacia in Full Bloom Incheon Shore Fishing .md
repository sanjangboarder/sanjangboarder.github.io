Hello, this is Sanjangborder.

It's been a while, but I went walking fishing in the Incheon area last weekend. Every year, around the time the acacia trees bloom, when the Fringe trees (also known as Chionanthus retusus) in the new towns are in full blossom, I consider it the official start of the rockfish walking season. When I lived in Seoul, I thought there were acacia trees everywhere, but after moving to Songdo, Incheon, it's hard to see acacias, and instead, I see the Fringe trees shown in the picture below as street trees all over the place.

Usually, the white flowers bloom properly in early to mid-May, and last week was the absolute peak, with great results. I didn't take any photos then, so I skipped the fishing report, but this time I managed to snap a few pictures to share the news about rockfish walking fishing in Incheon.

Walking fishing can be dangerous wherever you go, so I never go alone. So, after a long time, I gathered my Songdo crew and went rockfish walking fishing together. As expected, it offers a different kind of fun compared to boat fishing or fishing at dedicated ponds.

The typical size of rockfish you can expect in the Incheon area, including the Sihwa Seawall, is what we call 'aerek' (juvenile rockfish). Sizes between 10-20cm are the most common, and anything over the 23cm release size is considered decent. Around 30cm is a 'jackpot,' and around 40cm is an 'absolute jackpot.'

So, I set up my gear targeting decent sizes, specifically 25cm and above. My rod is a Hues T583A, originally an ajing (light game) rod. While it might struggle a bit to 'lift' a 30cm rockfish, it can easily handle a 25cm one. I also set up a light Daiwa Airity reel to minimize fatigue, and used a 0.8-gauge braided line directly connected.

It's a spot with a lot of snags, and bites often come from the break line, so I intentionally used a no-sinker rig. I used a 3/0 wide gap hook with Yamades Junior and a standard pink worm, and I only had one snag-up in 3 hours – I was totally happy! haha. The downside is that since it's a rig where the hook is hidden, I had 20-30% short bites or missed hooksets, but without snags, this approach seems fine.

🔗 [Half Lure Yamades 2 Special Worm Insert Sinker Version](https://naver.me/55114OIJ)

For a no-sinker rig, a 3/0 wide gap hook is a perfect match for Yamades Junior (3 inches), and a 4/0 size pairs well with the standard Yamades Special (4 inches).

Rockfish started biting one after another, practically on every cast. Looks small in the picture below, right? Haha. This one is about 23cm. The boundary between 'aerek' (juvenile rockfish) and adult rockfish is about 23cm, and that's when their coloring starts to change... The fish in the photo below still retains some of its juvenile coloring.

I was surprised by the continuous bites, one fish per cast. It felt like all the fish caught were over 23cm. Since I was using a Hues ajing rod, even fish of this size provided fantastic fighting feel. Catching juvenile rockfish is fine, but if they're over the release size, well, that says it all, right? Haha.

I even measured them with the Haedong measuring board. As I expected, a lot of fish were in the 23-25cm range.

The usual pattern was that long casts into the 'sinijil' (sand+mud) section rarely yielded rockfish bites, maybe just one occasionally. However, the front break line, where rock piles have collapsed, making the bottom snaggy and messy, definitely provided continuous bites.

The stone embankment points in the Incheon area and along the Sihwa Seawall actually show almost the same pattern. And the spot I went to that day was no exception.

I think I enjoyed it for about 3 hours... The biggest one was 30cm! Haha. This size is definitely considered a jackpot for shore lure fishing in the Incheon area.

Last week, a 38cm one was caught too! Haha. Unfortunately, I didn't see any bigger ones this time.

Perhaps because it had been a while since I went shore lure fishing in the Incheon area, time just flew by. We started around 9 AM and fished for a little over 3 hours. It started getting chilly, and after catching what felt like 30-40 rockfish, and starting to smell fishy, we decided to pack up and head out.

I considered taking a photo of the total catch, but I released the smaller ones immediately, not wanting to stress them, so I don't have one. I regret a little not taking pictures, especially since so many decent-sized fish came out.

It seems like the fishing will be good for a while, so I want to go again. However, it's a spot where you have to walk quite a distance after parking, and it can be dangerous to go without companions, so I'm not sure when I'll be able to go again. But if conditions allow, I'll be sure to share more updates on shore fishing in the Incheon area.
