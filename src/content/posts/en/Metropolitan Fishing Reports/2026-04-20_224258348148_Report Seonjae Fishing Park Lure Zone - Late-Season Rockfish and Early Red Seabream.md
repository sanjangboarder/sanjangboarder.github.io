​

Over the past weekend, temperatures rose to late-spring levels. Sea temperatures are rising accordingly. With Incheon area water temperatures reaching 12°C, the spring webfoot octopus season is active and monster flatfish are beginning to appear.

​

The water temperature at Seonjae Fishing Park in Seonjaedo, Incheon, operates differently from the open sea. It drops lower in cold weather and rises higher in warm weather due to the smaller volume of the artificial pond. On this trip, the water temperature in the Lure Zone Mini Float Pond sat between 16°C and 17°C.

​

At this time of year, day temperatures reach 25°C while night temperatures fall to the low 10s, showing a high diurnal temperature range. As temperatures rise, rockfish are completing their spawning cycle and moving past their active feeding temperatures, while red seabream activity is starting to increase in the warming pond water.

​

This transition marks the start of red seabream season. Since I prefer rockfish over red seabream, I visited Seonjae Fishing Park to target late-season rockfish. I prepared a portable aerator to transport live shrimp, along with warm clothing for the evening.

I used live loaches and live shrimp as bait. Loaches appeal to rockfish and draw attention from red seabream; they serve as a practical multi-purpose bait. I also brought live shrimp to supplement the layout.

​

Live shrimp require water temperatures above 14°C to remain active, and they survive longer once temperatures exceed 16°C. They perform best in 20°C water. Although it was early in the season, they drew more interest from fish compared to loaches.

​

I carried the live shrimp in a Haedong 9-liter eging cooler fitted with an aerator. Utilizing a dedicated scoop net simplified baiting.

​

Anglers reported that short pitching casts had produced no daytime activity, with occasional hits occurring at a depth of 2m. I targeted Net Cage No. 1. Because rockfish gather around structural cages, I expected a hit, but did not receive a bite. While rockfish responded in water temperatures below 10°C, they avoided the bait during this session.

​

Red seabream maintain culinary quality through May, after which their flavor decreases. While I prefer other species for the table, their fighting strength is notable.

​

With rising water temperatures, I plan to focus on charter boat and shore rock fishing trips rather than artificial ponds. During warmer months, mosquitoes, sandflies, and blackflies are common around the ponds, making night fishing less comfortable. In the upcoming weeks, I will share updates on spring webfoot octopus, charter gathering, and flatfish trips.
