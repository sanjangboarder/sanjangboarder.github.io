With the webfoot octopus and cuttlefish season open, I have been preparing gear for weekly trips. When rigging for webfoot octopus, many experienced anglers use branch-line rigs (leader rigs). Today, I review a product designed for cuttlefish that performs well for webfoot octopus: the **Haedong Cuttlefish Long Type Rig**.

​

The package layout is shown in the image below. You can locate it online by searching for the manufacturer model code **HA-1898**.

The rig uses small swivels and snaps suitable for West Sea boat drifts, which limits drag and supports sensitivity. Because most modern jigs integrate rolling swivels, the jig attachment point uses a simple snap without an additional swivel. The sinker connection utilizes a snap-lock rolling swivel, which is robust enough to protect high-end tungsten sinkers from break-offs.

​

The design utilizes a cross-hole bead system. To extend the branch line, you tie a new, longer carbon line to the bead. To shorten it, you pull the line through the bead to your preferred length, tie a new overhand knot, and cut the excess line.

​

To shorten the branch line, pull the line to shorten the lead, tie a lock knot, and cut the tail end.

The photo on the left displays the default setup with a sinker and jig attached. The photo on the right shows a modified configuration with a shortened branch line. Shortening the branch line takes about 10 seconds, allowing for quick adjustments on the water. Extending the line requires replacing the lead, which demands additional time and spare line, though the cross-hole bead simplifies the process.

For cuttlefish drifts, I utilize tungsten sinkers. The interlock swivel is robust and guards against losing sinkers. The jig connection uses a small snap that keeps weight to a minimum, allowing the jig to maintain a horizontal profile on the fall.

​

Replacing the sinker with a weighted jig (aeja) converts the setup into a webfoot octopus branch-line rig. During my recent outings, I fished this configuration with a slightly shortened dropper while maintaining the branch line, and it produced consistent strikes from webfoot octopus.

I previously tied custom rigs that mirrored this design. Using this pre-tied version saves time, eliminating the need to tie multiple rigs the night before a trip.

​

While tying custom rigs is part of preparation, using a reliable pre-tied option is practical. I am testing this rig on my upcoming trip to Ocheon Port.
