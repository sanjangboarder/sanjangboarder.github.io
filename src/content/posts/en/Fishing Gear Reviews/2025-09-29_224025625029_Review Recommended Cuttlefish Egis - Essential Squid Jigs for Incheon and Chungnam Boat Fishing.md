Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

While I anticipated moderate results for webfoot octopus this year, the actual counts have been high. The Chungnam region continues to see stable catches. In Incheon, where tidal changes significantly affect results, anglers faced challenges during the maximum spring tide early in the season, but counts recovered to moderate levels as the tidal cycles stabilized. Catch rates in Gunsan and Hongwon Port are also higher compared to previous years.

​

Overall, the West Coast has shown active feeding patterns. When boat fishing on the West Coast, the primary grounds are in Chungnam, including Cheonsuman Bay, followed by Incheon and Gunsan. Anglers who targeted webfoot octopus early in the season have likely secured their target numbers. Personally, I have passed the 600 mark and am transitioning my tackle setup to target cuttlefish.

​

The 2025 market for webfoot octopus and cuttlefish jigs (egis) is highly competitive. Jigs priced around 3,000 KRW have shown standardization in performance and quality. This means most jigs on the market can produce basic results. Nonetheless, it is of interest to see which designs will stand out during the peak season.

​

In this post, I will introduce jigs that have consistently performed during West Coast boat fishing trips for cuttlefish. These are models that established their reliability through the previous season, selected based on my own logs. Experienced anglers will recognize most of these models, while beginners can use this list to understand what patterns to include in their rotations.

​

Through my cuttlefish sessions, I have noted that no single jig pattern is effective under all parameters, such as weather, water clarity, or depth. Matching the jig to the current conditions is required. Consequently, rotating your jigs is key. If other anglers nearby are landing fish consistently, it is advisable to match their jig color or pattern.

​

Understanding which jigs experienced anglers prioritize is helpful. During my trips, I have noted the models used by top anglers, including designs that I lacked during active feeding windows.

### **Yamashita Toto Sutte AJ**

​

While this is not my personal go-to jig, I always keep it in my box. On certain occasions, it performs highly. The AJ pattern (representing horse mackerel) is widely recognized. It is effective in specific conditions and remains an important part of a rotation. The QS color in the Yamashita Toto Sutte line is also used, though I have not observed consistent results with it.

​

The AJ model was released in a smaller size this year. The smaller profile can be advantageous through late September. As the main cuttlefish season starts in October, the standard size becomes functional.

​

You do not need to stock large numbers of this model; one or two units are sufficient. It shares pattern characteristics with local army green or target shrimp jigs.

### **Yo-Zuri Tinsel L6 Size 3**

The tinsel model performs best when the current is moving, particularly when the boat drifts at around 1.0 knot. Under these conditions, it produces consistent responses. When the current is active, I typically start my session with this jig.

### **Yo-Zuri Sam-bong Purple KVPU**

​

This is a bait-wrapped jig design. Increased supply this season has reduced its retail price, making it more accessible. Its application is specific: it is used as a test jig when transitioning to deep-water spots. A smaller version was introduced this season.

​

While various purple patterns are available with similar response rates, the KVPU color is cost-effective. The standard size is relatively large, making it more suitable for use from October onward. While it is unclear if deep-sea cuttlefish outings will be as common in the Shinjindo area this season, this jig remains required for those spots.
