Hello, this is Sanjang Border.

This year, with the West Sea boat fishing season starting again, I've had a slightly different thought. Until last year, I mainly used #1 braided line, and for webfoot octopus and cuttlefish, I used #0.8. Thanks to that, line flutter was reduced, and it performed well in sensitive conditions, but the only drawback was the strength of the #0.8 or #1 braided line.

I wished it were just a little stronger. When boat fishing for webfoot octopus and cuttlefish these days, I often use high-quality, relatively expensive jigs, so I wondered if increasing the strength, even if it means a slightly thicker line, might be a good idea. Also, I've already caught 7 large flatfish this year, and I have a feeling I'll catch sea bream over 50cm, so I wanted to increase the strength of the braided line on my light jigging and Tai Rubber reels from #1.

So, I additionally acquired #1.2 and #1.5 braided lines and spooled them onto a new baitcasting reel I bought. I'd like to talk about that.

This is a braided line I discovered two years ago: Haedong HDF's Limit X9 9-strand braided fishing line. It's definitely made thinner compared to low-cost or entry-level braided lines, and not only is the strength similar, but it's also very smooth, which makes me very satisfied.

Let's take a detailed look at what the #1.2 and #1.5 braided lines are like.

First, the packaging. It comes spooled with #1.2, 200m, and has a stated strength of 23lb. Since it can withstand approximately 10.75kg of strength, it's difficult for a forced retrieve of a meter-class large flatfish, but it has enough strength to almost forcibly pull in fish up to 80-90cm.

I directly measured the thickness of the #1.2 9-strand braided line. The specification states 0.185mm, but my digital caliper measures it at 0.162mm. When measuring low-cost braided lines, the results are similar to the specifications, but Haedong Limit X9 braided line can definitely be considered thinner.

If I were to increase the strength to 15kg, it would require going beyond #2, and I didn't want it to be too thick, so I prepared a #1.5 braided line for this purpose.

Here's the Little Giant spool with 100m of #1.5 braided line spooled on it. If spooled completely, it would probably hold about 150m, but as I mentioned earlier, I recommend spooling only 100m to save line and make it easier to replace old line. Unless you're doing fishing that involves casting a lot of line, like Tai Rubber, or frequently fishing depths beyond 50m, 100m is sufficient for a full season.
