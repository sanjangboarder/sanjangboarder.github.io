Standard practice involves a quick freshwater rinse or running tap water over the gear. To protect my tackle, I acquired the **Haedong Salt Neutralizer Spray**.

​

The product features a spray-trigger bottle designed for easy application. It is available in three volumes: 120ml, 250ml, and 500ml. I purchased the 120ml version to test its performance. This compact size fits well for home use and can also be carried along to wash gear immediately at the port.

​

According to the specifications, the formula relies on a plant-based decomposition agent, making it eco-friendly. It also contains deodorizing agents and moisturizers to prevent surface wear, which should assist in removing bait and saltwater odors.

The directions state that you can spray the solution onto the gear at the port and wipe it clean with a towel. However, I integrated it into my standard home washing routine.

​

I positioned my spinning and baitcasting reels in the sink, sprayed the solution evenly, allowed it to sit for a few minutes, and then rinsed them under running water while rotating the handle. While the immediate visual difference compared to using water alone is subtle, the spray appears to neutralize odors effectively.

​

When washing reels, it is important to use cold water. Unlike fishing rods, reels contain lubricants, oils, and greases inside their gear assemblies and bearings. Washing with warm water can dissolve these lubricants, causing them to leak from the housings.

​

Additionally, many reels feature drain holes at the bottom of the frame. Washing a reel upside down or submerging it in water can force water into these ports, which can degrade the internal gears.

​

Keep these two guidelines in mind: wash the reel in its upright position, and use cold water. Applying the salt neutralizer alongside these steps completes a proper maintenance routine.

​

Washing fishing rods is simpler. I sprayed the salt neutralizer over the entire blank, focusing on the line guides. The carbon composite blanks do not rust, but the guide frames can develop corrosion if constructed from budget metal alloys. Paying attention to the guides and reel seat threads helps extend their lifespan.

​

I have used the salt neutralizer for about a month, and it shows differences compared to rinsing with water alone. Guide corrosion has been reduced, and the gear grinding sensation that occasionally occurred during the first retrieve after storage has been resolved.

​

Using a specialized maintenance spray supports effective salt removal. For an angler fishing once a week, the 120ml bottle should last approximately two months. I plan to use this bottle through the season and purchase a larger volume in the future.

​

High-end gear typically features corrosion-resistant components, but proper care is still recommended to maintain its condition. For entry-level or cost-effective gear priced under 100,000 KRW, the guide frames and reel components are often susceptible to rust. Applying a salt neutralizer can notably influence the durability and long-term performance of these setups.
