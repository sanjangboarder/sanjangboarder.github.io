Now, let's look at the rod itself. It's a 2-piece rod, but not a center-cut design; instead, it separates just in front of the reel seat. While 2-piece rods with a center-cut design offer the advantage of smaller bulk for portability, it's challenging to bring out 100% of the rod's flex characteristics. By separating it in front of the reel seat, the flex can be maximized naturally in the first section, which means a slight compromise on portability. It's good to understand that most high-end rods come in this form.

The guides feature Titanium and FUJI labeling, giving them a trustworthy appearance. Looking at the single-foot guides, I pondered their pros and cons. The lowest guide is typically the part that bears the most load due to the rod's structure, which is why double-foot guides, better for handling heavy loads, are usually adopted. While single-foot guides might be slightly less advantageous for bearing heavy loads, the section where they are wrapped and epoxied onto the rod is much shorter compared to double-foot guides, which is more beneficial for expressing the rod's inherent flex. Hence, I suspect this might be a new attempt or approach.

So, I wondered how they minimized the durability disadvantage of single-foot guides compared to double-foot guides. Looking at the guide height, it's a little less than 2cm. Significantly low single-foot guides have been used compared to the guide ring height on spinning rods. In other words, if the single-foot guides were taller, the disadvantages in terms of twisting or durability might have outweighed the advantages, but it seems they've found the optimal height through various tests. In the picture below, I compared the height of the last two guides on two of my Tai Rubber rods, and one was 1.2cm and the other 1.9cm, showing no significant difference. I've now thoroughly examined the Aomax Navigator. I own three Tai Rubber rods of mid to high-end quality, and compared to my existing rods, its distinct advantages and differentiators are clear. To summarize, there are three main points:

**1. Ultra-Lightweight**
The recent trend in fishing rods is lightweight design. Making a rod lightweight is becoming a fundamental requirement for high-end rods, and the reason is that it's not just about making one component lighter—like the blank (rod body), guides, or reel seat—but rather, these elements are organically intertwined.
First, although the blank material isn't specified in the product details, I heard at the fishing expo that it uses top-grade materials from Toray, such as T1100 and M40X. While technical details on how these were mixed and in what ratios aren't disclosed, you can assume that it fundamentally uses the best carbon available. So, what are the characteristics of top-grade carbon? It's that even when made lightweight, it ensures recovery power and elasticity. In other words, it's a magical material that allows for both lightness and excellent rod characteristics, but the only problem is that it's expensive.
And by adopting a carbon reel seat, it will naturally be light relative to its rigidity. The guides also utilize Titanium SIC. In short, you can correctly assume that all the components making up this rod are made from top-tier materials.

**2. High-Modulus Carbon Full Solid with Soft Characteristics**
If simply using good carbon material were enough to call something high-end, then every brand could easily produce high-end rods. The Aomax product description states that technologies like PCA and HIGH BROAD, shown below, have been applied. You can consider this a design aimed at maximizing the material's characteristics. Personally, I always thought high-end rods should ideally be high-modulus carbon full solids, and this rod turned out to be exactly that.

**3. Novel Guide Placement Concept**
After thoroughly examining the rod, one of the most impressive aspects was the two guides closest to the reel seat. They are single-foot guides, typically used on spinning reels. At first, I was puzzled, wondering if this made sense and why they chose it. Theoretically, the guides near the reel seat bear the most load when a large fish is hooked, so double-foot guides, commonly used with baitcasting reels, would naturally be the choice for twisting and durability. But to use single-foot guides, primarily seen on spinning reels? It seemed a bit strange, but I believe it's a completely new attempt at a design that maximizes the rod's flex characteristics. I'm really curious to see what kind of feel and satisfying flex it will demonstrate in actual fishing conditions.
