Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

Last year, Creman released a compact round baitcasting reel. I examined it at the Korea International Fishing Expo earlier this year and found the design highly appealing. It was consistently out of stock due to limited production runs, but water volumes opened up last month and I purchased a unit. The retail price sits in the mid-300,000 KRW range.

​

This price matches the historic launch price of the Doyo Coba 3 Gold. Creman has now released the Coba 4 Gold. If I had not purchased this model, I would likely have chosen the Coba 4 Gold, as I prefer gold styling on round reels.

​

Like my experience with the Creman A9 Plus, this model ships in a hard shell case. While it protects the reel during shipping, I have never seen an angler use this case on deck during ten years of boat fishing. I would prefer standard cardboard packaging with a corresponding price reduction.

​

Opening the hard shell case reveals the reel wrapped inside an additional soft pouch.

### Specifications and Size Profile

The specifications state a 15kg drag capacity. Based on field tests targeting flatfish and cuttlefish, the drag is strong for a compact reel, though confirming the full 15kg rating is difficult. In the West Sea, it offers excess drag power. The double-handle model includes 11 ball bearings, and uses a 5.6:1 gear ratio.

​

​

The weight is a key highlight. At 161g on paper for a metal body reel, it is very light.

​

My scales measured **163.3g** for the double-handle configuration, which is very light.

​

This weight is achieved via its compact size. The reel profile is smaller than standard baitcasting reels.

The spool includes a magnetic brake rotor. The adjustment is limited to three basic settings. For vertical boat fishing, fine magnetic brake tuning is unnecessary, so this is not a major issue.

While I am satisfied with the weight and design, I noted some areas for improvement. During the first trip, a faint rubbing noise was audible under retrieve, likely caused by clearance tolerances between the side cover and the spool. However, the noise disappeared after landing three fish, including the large flatfish.

​

I noted a similar break-in period with my Creman A9 Plus reel, where the initial retrieve feel improved after several trips. This suggests that final machining tolerances for smooth retrieves could be improved, though they resolve with use.

This season, I will continue using the Creman Little Giant on setups utilizing Size 40 sinkers for flatfish, sea bass, white croaker, and rockfish. I will report on its long-term durability in my upcoming logs. If you prefer gold styling, this reel is a solid choice.
