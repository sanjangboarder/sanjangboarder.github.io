Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

While float fishing is not the most dominant technique in the metropolitan Incheon area, it has a dedicated following along the Sihwa Seawall, Songdo, and Yeongjongdo. The primary target species here are rockfish and greenling. Because the West Sea seabed is highly structured and littered with snags, set depths can occasionally be misjudged, causing sinkers or hooks to snag and snap the line.

​

This also occurs frequently at metropolitan saltwater pay-fishing ponds that I visit, such as Seonjae Fishing Park, Manjeong Mint, Jeoseong, and Daebudo. Floats represent the most expensive component in float fishing rigs: cheap spindle (dome) floats or stick floats start at 3,000 KRW, while premium electronic models exceed 10,000 KRW. Losing a float when a line snaps is frustrating.

​

To recover floats after a break, a specialized tool called a **float retriever** is highly useful. Today, I review HDF's model, shown below.

Some alternative retrievers feature central buoyancy foam, though I find this unnecessary. Overly complex retrievers are more prone to damage, causing inconvenience during trips.

​

I snapped a float at Seonjae Fishing Park last week, but was not concerned since I had the HDF retriever. The float had drifted roughly 20m out in the wind. The key to using a retriever is accurate casting.

​

Using an overhead cast, throw past the target float, align your retrieve direction, and reel in. The prongs will scoop the floating target as shown below. The recovery process is surprisingly fun.

Having float-fished for many years, I have tested various retrievers. The HDF model is one of the most reliable and simple designs on the market. Retrieving environments are often harsh, making durability critical. Its simple structure prevents mechanical failures, and it casts over 20m comfortably.

​

Priced affordably, it is a highly recommended accessory to keep in your tackle box to avoid losing expensive floats.
