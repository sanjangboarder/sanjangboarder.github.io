Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

This year, Seonjae Fishing Park in Incheon added a new "Mini-Float Pond" inside their Lure Zone. Because this is a compact pond rather than an open-water style pay-pond, standard 4.3m, 5.0m, or 5.3m rock/float fishing rods are prohibited. Only rods under 3m are permitted.

​

The equipment regulations for Seonjae's Mini-Float Pond are as follows:

​

> **"Lure or standard rods must be under 3.0m, and hand-line poles under 7.0m."**
>
> *From the Seonjae Fishing Park Cafe Announcement*

​

This regulation directed my attention toward short standard rods. Anglers typically use 2-piece inshore lure casting rods, eging rods, or rockfish/mebaru rods under 9 feet. Lure rods are generally stiffer than rock/float rods. This stiffness is designed to facilitate lure actions and cast weighted lures. While fun, lure rods lack the supple, bendy action of float rods that makes fighting fish on float setups highly satisfying.

​

Having float-fished along the Sihwa Seawall and Songdo for many years, I searched for a dedicated float rod under 3.0m.

​

🔗 [AliExpress Rock Fishing Rod Review (Naver)](https://blog.naver.com/sanjangboarder/222854193512)

I have several light rock-fishing rods that I enjoy using, but they measure 3.3m or 3.8m. To find a model measuring exactly 3.0m, I searched domestic manufacturers but found no options matching rock-fishing rod specifications. Fortunately, I discovered a suitable option on AliExpress. It serves as a good match for Seonjae's Mini-Float Zone.

For float rods, balance is critical. The 43cm measurement represents the distance between the reel seat and the rod's center of gravity.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA0MDVfMTA4/MDAxNzc1ODMzNDIxMzYx.mwE-53hz0BywIWKlVCIYwIhzFnPf38iuz7o2XjoMfDMg.gUojx-g1zWEW4pev_lvNfkRuPUwpvFPEJrA27HHFdQMg.JPEG/SE-d4894739-29bd-11f1-87ec-47ccc7c52a60.jpg?type=w800)

​

Mounted with a Shimano Stella 3000, the center of gravity shifts forward from the reel seat. The ideal balance point sits closer to the reel foot, which this setup does not achieve, resulting in some tip-heaviness.

​

This imbalance likely stems from the heavy guides. Using smaller, lighter rock-fishing guides would improve the balance.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjAzMzBfMTQx/MDAxNzc0ODMzNDQzMzMx.eGmVIUeSUuDKYt-shm2VSCRolZfUEBieiBAkYjC8x1og.J7rp-O0yw0XnHuCDu1o3lzBaVnXkHfp1l0M8UnX_qXsg.JPEG/SE-d48a7fbb-29bd-11f1-87ec-f912f7f19538.jpg?type=w800)

Currently, this is one of the few rods under 3m that carries standard rock-fishing characteristics. It provides a more supple, responsive action when fighting fish compared to standard lure casting rods. Priced affordably, it serves as a good alternative to lure rods.

​

The main drawback is the heavy guide setup. While upgrading the guides is possible, it adds cost, so I plan to run it stock until a guide replacement becomes necessary.

​

Additionally, the guides are prone to rust if not washed with fresh water after saltwater use. With proper rinsing, the rod should last. It is a versatile choice for Spanish mackerel, chub mackerel, horse mackerel, rockfish, and greenling.

​

### Field Test Impressions

​

Testing the rod at Seonjae Fishing Park's Mini-Float Pond confirmed it behaves similarly to a light surf rod, differing from carbon lure casting setups. Given its roughly 40,000 KRW price point, a major advantage is the telescopic design: being able to keep the rod rigged allowed me to start fishing in under a minute. At 3.0m, the rod offers some flex, but remains stiffer than standard light inshore lure rods. Casting weighted floats was efficient, and the 3m length provided good leverage when hook-setting and landing fish.

​

However, lure casting rods offer better sensitivity and lighter weights because of their higher-modulus blank designs. The 157g weight of this rod causes some fatigue over long sessions, requiring me to use rod holders periodically.
