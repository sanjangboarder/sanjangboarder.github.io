As a field staff member for Haedong Tackle, I suggested the concept for a 2-tier branch-line rig designed for casual coastal fishing in the West Sea. I am happy to see this idea transition into a commercial product.

​

<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNTEyMjVfMjMy/MDAxNzY2NjI4Nzg3MTQ5.czEGrgo5utlGFdJaniro_cg47jzXQbOJmuUAkU8FeuUg.Uk5L-_WNLAbzuM9NWzMy0kdNT5ymUpqN3lBHGNCS1vgg.PNG/image.png?type=w800" alt="Draft diagram of 2-tier branch rig" />

​

The concept originated from my experience with boat fishing in the West Sea. When targeting rockfish, greenling, or white croaker in coastal waters, standard heavy-duty rockfish damping rigs are commonly used. However, these rigs are excessively stiff for the target species size. I saw a need for a rig with balanced line strength.

​

The production version of the Cha Pro All-In-One Rig did undergo minor adjustments to suit mass manufacturing requirements and expand target species. While the use of cross-beads and thinner carbon lines was maintained, the hook sizes were reduced. This adjustment makes the rig suitable for targeting rockfish, gold-striped rockfish, and chub mackerel in the South Sea.

Standard boat rigs often use thick nylon monofilament lines. In contrast, this rig uses a thin, high-strength fluorocarbon line, which improves sensitivity. Furthermore, unlike thick monofilament lines that are difficult to break when snagged on the bottom, the 20lb (No. 5) fluorocarbon line can be broken under tension to salvage the rest of your main line.

A Size 10 interlock snap swivel is used at the sinker end. The construction quality is solid. Given that lead sinkers are the most expensive expendable component on the rig, the use of an interlock snap to prevent accidental release is a sensible design detail.

​

​

The key feature of the rig is the use of cross-beads to connect the branch line. Cross-beads are not commonly used on commercial rigs but offer practical advantages. If a hook snags and breaks, the cross-bead configuration allows you to replace just the branch line rather than the entire rig. This design also permits easy adjustments to the branch line length based on fishing conditions.

​

While the clear fluorocarbon line is difficult to see, the overall structure is clean. When tension is applied by the sinker, the main line straightens while the branch line rotates freely via the cross-bead. This prevents line tangling and allows for a natural bait presentation.

​

Given the current winter season, testing this rig immediately would require a trip to the South Sea for rockfish or gold-striped rockfish. Since I reside in Incheon, organizing a southern trip is currently impractical. I plan to test the rig during the upcoming spring season for coastal rockfish and greenling.

​

While there are many rigs available for coastal boat fishing, multi-tier rigs utilizing cross-beads are uncommon. For targeting lighter species, the sensitivity and natural bait presentation offered by a cross-bead rig provide distinct benefits.

​

Additionally, I expect this rig to perform well in float fishing for chub mackerel. By combining it with a Size 1 or 2 stick float or dome float, and mounting a matching ringed sinker at the bottom, you can assemble a 2-hook float rig that covers different depths. This setup can also be adapted for bait fishing targeting horse mackerel or rockfish.

​

It should also prove effective for raft or cabin fishing in the West Sea. By matching the sinker weight to the tidal currents, the sensitive setup of this rig will help detect subtle bites.

​

I plan to use this rig regularly during my 2026 outings and will publish field reports as the season progresses.
