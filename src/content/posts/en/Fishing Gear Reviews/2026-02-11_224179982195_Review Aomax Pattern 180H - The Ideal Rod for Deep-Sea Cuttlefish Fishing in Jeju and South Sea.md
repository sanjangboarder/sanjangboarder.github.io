Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

The winter-to-spring deep-sea cuttlefish season in Jeju and the South Sea is currently at its peak. In 2026, we have received more notable catch reports compared to past years, raising my interest. I have planned trips to both Jeju and the South Sea, but weather constraints have delayed my departure.

​

However, setting up the tackle remains a prerequisite for deep-sea cuttlefish boat fishing. Today, I introduce a cuttlefish rod well-suited for the deep-sea season.

​

It is the **Pattern Deep-Sea Cuttlefish Rod** from **Aomax**. It is released in three configurations based on action: the B-180H and B-175MH featuring an 8:2 action, and the B-170XH featuring a 9:1 action. Today, I review the **B-180H** model.

Reviewing the specifications, the rod tip utilizes **Titanium (metal tip)**, while the blank utilizes T800 carbon fiber sheet. Crucially, the rod is **Made in Korea**, which is a welcome detail. The titanium tip allows a thin top diameter of 0.8mm and a bottom diameter of 8.5mm, representing a slim profile for a 1.8m rod. The design combines T800 carbon for durability with high-modulus carbon (30T/40T) to create a slim, lightweight rod. The total weight is 85g; while heavier than shallow-water octopus rods used in the West Sea, 85g is considered lightweight for a deep-sea rod designed to handle size 30–40 sinkers.

Let's examine the details of the Aomax Pattern Deep-Sea Cuttlefish Rod. It is a 2-piece configuration featuring a butt-joint design where the connection sits just in front of the reel seat, resulting in a long tip section. While this configuration increases the transport length, it offers advantages in sensitivity and action curve under load.

With the guard installed, the balance point sits just in front of the reel seat. Without the guard, the center of gravity shifts forward by approximately one finger's width. Given that deep-sea fishing requires using heavy sinkers that pull the tip down continuously during operation, this shift has a minor practical impact, but keeping the balance point at the reel seat using the guard is preferable.

The specified action is 8:2, though in my tests it felt closer to an 8.5:1.5 curve. With light size 20 sinkers, it acts like a 9:1 rod, while size 40 sinkers produce an 8.5:1.5 curve. On some titanium-tipped rods, the transition between the carbon blank and the metal tip can appear disjointed under load, but this rod demonstrates a smooth curve.
