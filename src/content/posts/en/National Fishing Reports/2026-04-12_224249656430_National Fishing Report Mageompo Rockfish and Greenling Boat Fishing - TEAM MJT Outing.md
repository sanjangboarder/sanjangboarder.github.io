Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

Mageompo is one of the premier destinations for West Sea boat-based rockfish fishing. For our TEAM MJT April monthly outing, we booked the **Jayeon Fishing-ho**, a highly regarded boat for target rockfish trips.

​

We were concerned about weather reports indicating rain and wind two days prior, but the weather at Mageompo looked manageable around departure time. The absence of sea fog and light winds raised our expectations, suggesting we might have escaped the bad weather window.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA0MTJfMjE1/MDAxNzc1OTcxNDEwMzMz.hgijR7q3UOGtPTHvXIOPa2HkPTjL3QrxpX0qzAmv5psg.kkwbANe4ToTH4BR_q963kikJE3bqxLo7Zvol_br5v0Yg.JPEG/900_20260411_052234.jpg?type=w800)

​

TEAM MJT members gathered promptly, and we were the first boat to clear Mageompo harbor. Before the giant flatfish (halibut) season begins, the Jayeon Fishing-ho targets artificial reefs and deep water zones using fresh bait for rockfish and greenling. Greenling and rockfish caught at this time of year taste outstanding, and everyone boarded hoping for a productive trip.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA0MTJfMjc4/MDAxNzc1OTcxNDA1NzUx.7jpjVHYfmAI8F7sDYBSsEYdwLL3xNSygnnpSY5PuEz8g.xV-GaE34-qwvTgot4xjfuktzWnODdq7eym83dW_P4O4g.JPEG/900_20260411_052810.jpg?type=w800)

​

Having received prior notice that we would target deep water zones around 40 to 50 meters, we prepared 150g to 180g (40–50호) sinkers and electric reels to handle the depth. I rigged two setups using JS Company's new **Liberty Ocean Light Jigging** rods (3-power and 4-power) and fished them with standard 2-hook bait rigs, bracket rigs, and down shot rigs.

​

> **Mageompo Boat Fishing Tackle & Rigs**
>
> **Setup 1:**
> *   **Rod:** JS Company Liberty Ocean B61-3
> *   **Reel:** Doyo Coba 3
> *   **Line:** Haedong X9 Limit PE 1.2

>

​

It was my first time using both the JS Company Liberty Ocean B61-3 and B61-4 rods. Positioned as budget-friendly rods for beginner and intermediate anglers, they performed satisfyingly over a full day of use. While I generally prefer fast, stiff rods, I favored the B61-3 model on this trip. Though slightly softer than the 4-power model, it delivered a crisp feel and excellent sensitivity, registering even the tiny nibbles of small yellowbelly rockfish.

​

The B61-4 model provided a heavier, stiffer feel, though this impression may have been influenced by the weight of the electric reel mounted to it.

​

While I did not hook any massive fish, I landed 40cm-class greenling on both rods. Winding and controlling the fish felt comfortable, suggesting the rods could handle giant flatfish under a meter without issue.

Our captain began fishing on the left side of Gauido Island and moved south as the day progressed. While we initially planned to run out to Gyeokbiyuldo Island, high swells and strong winds prevented us from heading that far out.

About an hour after leaving port, I stepped out of the cabin to find a thick blanket of sea fog covering the water. There was no fog in the forecast, but it remained heavy all day without clearing.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA0MTJfMTcy/MDAxNzc1OTc0OTc1MzAw.4HgfwtMg5QkRcQ2muwvEUebKLbf2tvhuiP8Ytvg4Zakg.zJBNmLzGncKHQTJN79iS965NjS42HF36lse8Db06VVMg.JPEG/SE-b47698fa-362f-11f1-88f4-435fc92da1ac.jpg?type=w800)

​

The ocean swells posed an even greater challenge. Heavy swells near a meter rolled throughout the day. While the video may not show the full intensity, maintaining balance on deck was difficult, making for challenging fishing conditions.

​

However, swells do not prevent you from fishing. We focused our efforts on landing rockfish and greenling.

​

On our first drift over artificial reefs, we lost several rigs to snags, which was frustrating. The captain quickly shifted us to rocky flats, which reduced snags and allowed us to start landing fish.

​

Our drifts covered areas holding both rockfish and greenling, as well as zones holding greenling exclusively. While the action was not fast, we received steady bites throughout the day.

Greenling bites were distinct, while rockfish often held the bait quietly without displaying a clear bite response, making them difficult to detect. We fished dyed squid strips as bait all day, which drew good responses from both species.

Despite the challenging conditions, everyone fished hard for the first few hours of the morning. By noon, however, several members were struggling with seasickness. Although our group consisted of experienced anglers, roughly a third struggled to adjust to the rolling swells.

Fortunately, I did not experience seasickness, though enjoying lunch proved difficult because of the heavy rolling of the boat. I ate a small portion to maintain my energy while avoiding stomach discomfort, drank some soda, and prepared for the afternoon session.

Fish continued to come aboard in the afternoon. The water temperatures remained low, resulting in catches of shaggy sea raven (samsigi) and a high proportion of greenling.

​

While I did not land any large rockfish, several fish in the 40cm to 50cm range were brought aboard by other members.

​

This is my final catch after fishing hard through the afternoon: a few rockfish, a shaggy sea raven, and greenling filling out the rest of the cooler. While I suspect we would have caught twice as many fish in better weather, it was a respectable catch given the challenging conditions.
