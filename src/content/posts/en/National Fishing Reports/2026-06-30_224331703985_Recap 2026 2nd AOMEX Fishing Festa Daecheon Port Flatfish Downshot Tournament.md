Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

Last weekend, I visited Daecheon Port to participate in the **2026 2nd AOMEX Flatfish Downshot FESTA**. While I missed the first event (the Tai Rubber Festa at Mageompo Port) due to a club schedule clash, I was able to clear my schedule to join this downshot tournament.

​

It had been a while since I competed in a fishing tournament. Knowing that many top-tier anglers and AOMEX creators would be participating, I joined with the simple goal of landing at least one big flatfish. Unlike other tackle brand tournaments, the entry fee for the AOMEX Festa was a budget-friendly 50,000 KRW. Furthermore, all catches from the tournament are donated to the *Fish Bank* charity to support good causes.

​

We were instructed to arrive at the tournament center by 3:30 AM. It was my first time visiting this gateball field location at Daecheon Port, and the setup was clean and professional. The AOMEX executive director briefed us on the event guidelines and charity goals, followed by the lottery draw for boat assignments. Shortly after 4:00 AM, we headed to our assigned boats to begin the competition.

The captain warned us in advance that while flatfish numbers were decent recently, the average size was small. To target larger fish, he planned to navigate all the way to Oeyeondo Island, which is a considerable distance from Daecheon Port. Running at fuel-efficient speeds, it would take roughly 2 hours. According to T-Map, we ran at roughly 28 km/h, flying Superman-style toward Oeyeondo.

Under tournament rules, competitors were required to fish using AOMEX-branded downshot rods. I fished with my newly acquired high-end downshot rod, the **AOMEX Navigator DS-180B**.

​

> *   **Worms**: Trigger X (TB, PP, PK, WH), Berkley HD
> *   **Sinker**: Size 40
> *   *SanjangBorder*

I paired the rod with my Daiwa Ryoga baitcasting reel, which has landed multiple large flatfish for me this season, and packed my most reliable Trigger X worm colors. Having landed about 7 large flatfish already this year, I hoped my good fortune would continue into the tournament.

The start was quiet. At the first spot, multiple hookup calls were heard, and fish began coming over the rail. However, the majority were around 40cm. Under tournament rules, flatfish under 40cm are released immediately, while larger fish are measured and kept in tanks.

​

An hour into the trip, 6 to 7 fish had been landed, but my line remained quiet. The smell of cooking food drifted over from the galley, announcing morning tteokbokki. It was a high-quality spread complete with fried seaweed rolls and boiled eggs—a satisfying breakfast.

The captain moved the boat consistently to locate active schools. Following the morning window, the action slowed to sporadic bites. My rod remained quiet. I rotated worm colors every 30 minutes, but had no response.

Since the Navigator is designed with a 6:4 moderate-fast action, lifting the sinker off the bottom creates the bend curve pictured below. The moderate-fast blank transmitted minor bottom structure bumps clearly, though I didn't get to experience a flatfish thump.

The *Superman* features a large iron griddle on the stern. Before lunch, the crew began cooking thick pork belly (samgyeopsal). I was amazed, as I had never seen a charter boat serve freshly griddled pork belly for lunch before. It was a massive quantity of pork belly, enough for 30–40 people, cooked alongside fresh rice prepared in pressure cookers on board.

I caught a small rockfish on a light bite, followed by a sharp strike that turned out to be a solid fat greenling.

​

The size of the greenling was excellent, giving me a brief moment of excitement before the head shakes confirmed the species.

​

This was my first fish on the Navigator downshot rod. Its moderate-fast blank was highly engaging to fish, bending smoothly under load. Fighting a large flatfish on this blank promises to be a lot of fun, and makes me want to plan another downshot trip.
