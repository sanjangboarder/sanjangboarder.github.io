Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

In a post late last year, I reviewed the AULA F87 Pro keyboard. Today, I present a review of its successor, the **F87 Pro V2**. Having used the original F87 Pro at home and the V2 at the office, I have compiled a comparison of their differences and upgrades.

​

> **AULA F87 Pro V2**

​

The AULA F87 Pro gained popularity as a cost-effective mechanical keyboard option and remains a steady seller. AULA has refined the design to introduce the Version 2 (V2) model.

​

Like the original F87 Pro, the V2 is released in multiple colorways. While pricing varies by option, the core specifications upgrades are listed below. The notable update is the dual 8,000Hz gaming mode, which increases the key polling rate to support faster response times during gaming. Other upgrades include a 12,000mAh battery (approximately three times the capacity of the original) and custom screen animation capabilities, alongside more vibrant LED lighting.

We will review the specification sheet across options. Option selection for the F87 Pro V2 is typically based on the keycap and switch configurations. I selected the **Transparent Black** model, which represents the highest specification tier. My review is based on this version.

​

The Transparent Black model features **Meteor switches**, a carbon positioning plate, and the dual 8,000Hz polling rate. The latency is rated at 0.125ms, making it a high-specification gaming keyboard option.

The keyboard keycaps are primarily transparent black. The design team placed distinct red accent keycaps on the 5, 6, 7, and 8 keys, alongside matching designs on Alt, Win, and spacebar modifiers.

​

While this layout has visual appeal, the design choices can be inconvenient. The color differences on the 5 and 8 keys are manageable, but the colored 6 and 7 keys complicate locating special characters above the number row during typing. Although spare keycaps are included, they do not include standard replacements for the number row. I adapted to this layout after a month of use, but it was initially disruptive.

Keyboard performance depends on registration accuracy and avoiding typos. A typing test showed speeds around 500 characters per minute with low error rates. While registration is consistent, I occasionally encountered double-key registrations. I identified this as a switch defect. After using the keyboard for a week, I swapped the problematic switches with the two provided spares, which resolved the issue. If you run out of spares, swapping defective switches with rarely used keys (*such as F1–F12*) is an alternative.
