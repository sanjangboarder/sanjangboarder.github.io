Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

It has been approximately 10 years since I first started flying drones. Between 2023 and 2025, I did not operate devices regularly, resulting in a loss of familiarity. However, recent releases from DJI caught my attention, prompting me to purchase a new model.

​

My previous drone ownership includes various toy models, an EHang Ghostdrone, a DJI Mavic Pro, and a Mavic Air. While I purchased a controller for FPV drones and practiced using simulators, I decided not to pursue FPV flight due to control complexity.

​

When considering my next device, FPV drones were my initial preference. However, due to their higher piloting difficulty, I have postponed that purchase, though I still practice occasionally.

​

My second preference was a sensor-equipped drone focused on stable aerial photography. Because I spend significant time fishing, I prefer a device that is practical to operate from a boat on the water. My criteria were: lightweight design, wind resistance, extended flight time, and a controller with an integrated LCD screen.

​

Within DJI's lineup, the Mini series fits these requirements. While larger models like the Air or Mavic offer higher camera specifications, they carry a weight penalty. Conversely, smaller models like the Neo or Flip are lighter but offer limited image quality and wind resistance.

​

The DJI Mini line has been established for over 5 years. While it is referred to as the "Mini 5," because of intermediate variant releases, it is more accurate to define it as the fifth-generation Mini platform.

​

The model I selected is the **DJI Mini 5 Pro Fly More Combo (with DJI RC 2)**. I will explain why I chose this configuration below.

​

The kit does not include a CPL filter, providing only ND8, ND32, and ND128 options. ND filters reduce the light entering the sensor; an ND8 filter cuts light transmission by a factor of 8, and an ND128 reduces it by a factor of 128. These are useful in bright environments like beaches or snowfields where excessive light causes the shutter speed to climb. Because the Mini 5 Pro features a fixed F1.8 aperture, exposure must be managed solely via shutter speed (up to 1/8000s).

​

While shutter speed alone manages exposure in standard conditions, bright summer or winter days occasionally require ND filters. Introducing an adjustable aperture would resolve this limitation, but DJI has reserved that feature for their Mavic Pro line, keeping fixed apertures on the Air and Mini series to differentiate the models.

Next is the **DJI RC 2** controller. DJI offers models that connect directly to a smartphone, alongside options featuring an integrated LCD screen like the RC 2. I recommend selecting the LCD-equipped controller.

​

Smartphone-dependent controllers require installing the "DJI Fly" app. Because this app is not distributed via the Google Play Store, users must sideload the APK file from DJI's website, which presents security and update concerns. Additionally, as Android OS versions update over the lifetime of the drone, app compatibility issues are common.

​

Using your phone as the screen also prevents you from using it for calls or messages while piloting, and the connection process is inconvenient. The integrated screen resolves these issues.

The camera utilizes a 3-axis mechanical gimbal to stabilize the image. Stabilization is divided into mechanical (gimbal) and electronic (EIS) systems. While DJI's electronic stabilization is effective, the mechanical gimbal provides a physical layer of isolation.

​

EIS can introduce motion blur or artifacts in low-light conditions, which the 3-axis gimbal helps prevent. However, since night flights after sunset are restricted under South Korean civil aviation laws and require separate authorization, low-light benefits are less critical for typical local operations.

The top shell of the drone houses obstacle detection sensors. Since the Mini 4 Pro, the series has featured 360-degree omnidirectional obstacle sensing, detecting objects in all directions. This reduces the risk of collisions.

However, pilots should not rely solely on the sensors. Small obstacles like tree branches or power lines are difficult for the sensors to resolve, and detection performance can vary under specific lighting conditions.
