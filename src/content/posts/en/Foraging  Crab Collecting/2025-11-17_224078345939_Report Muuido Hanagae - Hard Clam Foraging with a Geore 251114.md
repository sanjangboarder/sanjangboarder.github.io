I generally share foraging reports timed with spring tides when water levels drop significantly. In this post, I introduce a tidal flat foraging option in the Incheon area that is less dependent on spring tide conditions.

​

Our location is a popular spot in the Incheon area: Hanagae Beach on Muuido Island, which is accessible via a bridge from Yeongjongdo. I visited on the night of November 14.

​

Muuido Hanagae Beach features a high terrain elevation, meaning the water recedes far enough to allow clamming even on days that are not optimal spring tides. Because I visited on a Friday night during a non-spring tide period, few other foragers were present.

​

Low tide occurred at 6:12 PM with a tide height of 262 cm. During a negative spring tide, the level can drop to around -20 cm, meaning the water level on this day was over 2 meters higher than during a typical spring tide window. Foraging remains possible here even under these conditions.

​

Our target species was the hard clam. While one can search for hard clams using a standard hand hoe, finding them in open flats is challenging. A geore (a hand-dredge rake, shown below) is a more effective tool.

​

​

Using a geore involves dragging the rake along the sandy bottom, similar to a plow. When the tines strike a buried hard clam, the vibration is transmitted up the handle. Hanagae Beach consists primarily of sandy mud rather than deep, soft mud, allowing for easier walking while dragging the rake.

This dragging motion serves as physical exercise. When the rake clicks against an object, digging at that spot reveals a hard clam in most instances.

​

If you inspect the area where the rake catches, you can locate the clam. Digging at these spots turned up several large hard clams.

​

This method is more efficient than digging at random in search of buried clams.

​

I used the geore for approximately one hour. While the total number of clams was modest, it was sufficient for a family meal. Hard clam noodle soup recipes generally call for three to four clams per serving, so a catch of over 30 clams provides a reasonable quantity.

​

Hard clams purge sand when kept in clean seawater in a dark environment for two to three hours. I brought a plastic container and filled it with clean seawater before leaving the beach. This allows the clams to purge sand during the drive home, simplifying preparation.

​

The largest clam I collected was of significant size.

​

As we move into late November and early December, tidal flat foraging yields generally decrease. Winter foraging options include spoon worms or pen clams, though fishing in cold conditions presents challenges. The current season is winding down. With a few spring tides remaining in the year, foragers should plan their final outings safely.
