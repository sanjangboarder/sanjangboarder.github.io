This indicates that marine life will soon start moving in the low-tide zones of the Incheon region. For marine foraging (*collecting shellfish and crabs at low tide*), targeting the correct tide cycle is critical. I analyzed the March and April tide calendars for Incheon to identify the most productive periods.

​

<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjAzMTVfOTEg/MDAxNzczNTc2ODg1NTg0.nZtj5c17SmQJvWKXMndA4FUDNOAvM2g8wWruhCqQRrYg.a2o9ruKM-myfQsdw5WZHbQMwBa4hYVWMkNkqP_64n9Ig.PNG/image.png?type=w800" alt="Tide height graph" />

​

Spring and autumn are the peak seasons for low-tide foraging. During these seasons, the lowest low tides (*spring tides*) occur during the night. Consequently, nighttime is the primary window for foraging. Early March featured a spring tide on March 4, where the low tide height reached -17cm (*negative tide height*).

​

However, water temperatures during early March are too low to find active marine life. Anglers can only expect to dig up spoon worms (*Gaebul*).

​

​

A reasonable tide window occurs during the upcoming weekend. Specifically, the low tide on Friday night (which registers at 00:21 AM on Saturday) drops to -6cm. With water temperatures around 7°C to 8°C, moon snails (*whelks / Golbaengi*) should start appearing. However, they will not be highly active, so expectations should be moderated.

​

​

The spring tide in early April is less favorable. The maximum tide range percentage is 85%, which is average for foraging purposes.

​

On April 2, the lowest low tide level is around 27cm to 28cm. While foraging is possible, it is less optimal than the negative spring tides available during March and late April. Although water temperatures will be higher, the tidal reach is limited.

​

​

The peak tide window for April occurs on Friday night, April 17, when the low water level drops to 8cm. Although it is not a negative tide, it drops below the 40cm threshold on a Friday night. A low water level of 8cm exposes deep sand flats that have been inaccessible all winter, offering good potential. By this date, water temperatures should exceed 10°C, increasing the activity of moon snails and bringing conch (*Sora*) into shallower water. Anglers may also find small octopus (*Nakji*).

​

​

While daytime foraging is safer on the West Sea sand flats, nighttime foraging typically yields larger catches due to the nocturnal habits of target species. I recommend prioritizing dates that align with favorable nighttime low tides.

​

<div class="single-image">
<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjAzMTVfNzMg/MDAxNzczNTc2NDkyMjE4.8BWMPSKkWHlt2h8sydV_Gl-Za-eggcOuIFrrV2xpw9wg.zFldT3lDlhAhc2yajmYsn1f8HHcId9ThzGn5wIc8Od0g.PNG/image.png?type=w800" alt="Foraging schedule calendar" />
</div>

​

April marks the start of the season for moon snails, conch, spawning webfoot octopus, spoon worms, and small octopus. Popular flats in the Incheon and Gyeonggi regions, such as Seonnyeo Rock in Yeongjongdo, Hanagae Beach in Muuido, Sihwa Seawall, Daebudo, Seonjaedo, and Yeonghugdo, will see increased activity.

​

Spring foraging in the Incheon region is less crowded, and many visit to enjoy the outdoors at the start of the season. I recommend focusing on safety rather than target counts, treating any catch as a bonus.
