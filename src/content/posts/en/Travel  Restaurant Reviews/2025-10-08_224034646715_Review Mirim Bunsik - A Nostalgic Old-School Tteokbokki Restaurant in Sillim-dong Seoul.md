Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

During the recent Chuseok holiday, I visited a local eatery while staying at my mother's home. It is a bunsik-style (snack shop) restaurant.

​

This is a tteokbokki joint I have visited since my childhood when I lived in Sillim-dong, Seoul. While I occasionally crave its taste, I rarely had opportunities to visit due to limited parking in the area. Since it was only a five-minute walk from my mother's house, I took the time to revisit this location.

​

​

The name Mirim Bunsik is derived from Mirim Girls' High School, located a couple of minutes away. Given that Mirim Girls' High School opened in 1988, the restaurant has likely operated since around that time, representing over 30 years of business.

​

Walking down along the high school's retaining wall, you will see the building shown below across the street. The shop lacks a standard sign, displaying the name "Mirim Bunsik" only on the glass door panels.

​

Operating hours are from 11:00 AM to 8:00 PM, covering lunch and dinner service.

​

​

The interior retains the aesthetic of a classic establishment. The layout, tables, floor patterns, and portable gas burners are identical to what I recall from my elementary and middle school years. The walls have been repainted; previously, the dining alcove with floor seating was covered in customer graffiti.

​

​

The menu layout remains consistent with the style used in past decades. Although prices have adjusted over time, the items remain inexpensive.

​

For two diners, ordering a single portion of tteokbokki, a noodle add-on, and a side of kimchi fried rice is typically sufficient, totaling approximately 10,000 KRW.

​

Adding fried dumplings (2,000 KRW) and fish cake soup (4,000 KRW) brings the total to 16,000 KRW, providing a substantial meal for two.

​

​

Another menu panel displays noodle options. Anglers should note the instructions for the spicy cold chewy noodles (jjolmyeon) and cold noodles, which I will cover below.

​

Since my childhood visits, water, pickled radish, and utensils have been self-service. The water is served cold in reused 2-liter PET bottles, which is a characteristic detail of this shop.

​

​

Visiting as a family of four, I ordered two portions of tteokbokki, two ramen noodle additions, and one jjolmyeon noodle addition. The setup is prepared at the table as shown below.

​

<div class="single-image">
<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNTEwMDZfMjY1/MDAxNzU5NzEyNzk2MDIy.c-uzbPe_C9RFVTaQomXKrxbYaq-om1fl16aP9sM3730g.K2w3-7jL5CjO3wcpllmE__JMqjJ7BcnTbjMcFWkHPbcg.JPEG/900%EF%BC%BF20251005%EF%BC%BF121201.jpg?type=w800" alt="Instant tteokbokki pot on burner" />

​

Because the ingredients are starch-based, the dish cooks quickly. While the components are standard, the sauce has a distinct flavor that differs from Sincheon-dong or modern franchise styles.

​

The tteokbokki is ready in a few minutes. The ramen and jjolmyeon noodles should be eaten first to prevent them from overcooking and sticking to the pan. Once the noodles are cooked, reducing the burner to low is recommended.

​

​

The rice cakes are made of wheat flour (mil-tteok), offering a soft, chewy texture. Eating them with the broth and cabbage using a spoon is the recommended method. The spiciness is mild and accessible, focusing on savory qualities rather than heat.

​

Normally, ordering fried rice to mix with the remaining sauce is typical, but due to the large portion size, I had to skip it. I recommend ordering the fried rice if space permits.

The broth is flavorful, with a distinct black pepper element that prompts continued drinking. The fish cakes retain a firm texture, pairing well with the tteokbokki.

​

The fried dumplings are standard, fried to a golden color. They are best enjoyed when dipped in the accompanying dark soy sauce.

​

Here is the jjolmyeon, which is a signature dish of the restaurant.

​

The flavor is sour and sweet, with the sour profile being prominent. This sour balance is the reason the menu notes that diners should request adjustments beforehand if they dislike highly sour or sweet profiles. I prefer the standard preparation, which has a distinct character.

​

​

Mirim Bunsik has been a consistent dining choice for me for over three decades.

​

I would rate this restaurant **4.6 out of 5 stars**. It remains a reliable destination to visit. While the location is somewhat out of the way, it continues to attract a regular flow of customers.

​

It represents a relevant stop for enthusiasts of classic tteokbokki styles, and a practical dining option for those seeking retro-style food options in the area.

Thank you.
