Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

Following my previous post about pork soup, I visited Kkangtong Market to walk around and digest my meal. In the Nampo-dong area beyond Yeongdo in Busan, there are several traditional markets. While Jagalchi Market and Gukje Market were historically the most famous, Kkangtong Market has recently been frequently mentioned as a food destination, so I made it my primary target.

​

I arrived assuming parking would be simple, but locating a space was difficult. Parking close to the market was impossible; I drove around the perimeter and eventually parked in a private garage before entering.

​

Opposite Kkangtong Market lies Gukje Market. I entered there first and then crossed over.

The first snack destination I visited in Kkangtong Market was **Wooseong Butcher** (*Wooseong Sik-yuk*). Judging by the name, it originally operated as a standard meat shop.

​

It has transitioned into a specialized Gimbap shop after its pork belly and beef brisket Gimbap (referred to as *Sambak Gimbap*) was featured on tvN. The menu consists of two items: pork belly Gimbap and beef brisket Gimbap.

​

The operation is divided: the owner's wife rolls the Gimbap while the husband grills the meats. I visited on the afternoon of the final day of the Lunar New Year holiday, and the crowd was dense. The wait was approximately 20 minutes; the assembly line did not appear optimized for speed.

The ingredients for both the pork belly and beef brisket options are similar: green chili pepper, perilla leaves, carrots, onion, and lettuce. The pork belly option is dressed with ssamjang (seasoned soybean paste) sauce, while the beef brisket uses a different savory dressing that coordinates well.

​

I ordered one roll of each. They were sliced and packed as shown below.

The taste resembles eating grilled pork wrapped in lettuce with rice and ssamjang, with an added note of savory seaweed. The beef brisket option offers a similar experience. The flavor profile is distinct from standard Gimbap, which explains its popularity. I rate this stall **3.8 out of 5 stars**. It is worth trying if you walk past it.

​

Busan's traditional markets offer many local snacks beyond those reviewed here. These include street options like Bibim-dangmyeon (spicy mixed glass noodles) and Mul-tteok (skewered rice cake cooked in dashi), which are characteristic of Busan. Having tried them on previous trips, I focused on new items this time. I restricted my consumption to leave room for dinner. In the next post, I will share my review of the most satisfying restaurant from this trip to Busan.
