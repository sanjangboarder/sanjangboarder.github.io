Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

Although I have returned from my trip to Busan, I am continuing to share reviews of the restaurants I visited. While Busan is famous for many representative local foods, I had never tried Milmyeon (cold wheat noodles). This time, I visited a restaurant recommended by a local friend who knows the city's food scene.

​

The restaurant is located near Jung-dong Station in Haeundae. If your accommodation is in Haeundae, it is within walking distance.

​

The location is slightly away from the main tourist strip of Haeundae, situated along a main road with moderate foot traffic. However, it appears to be a popular local establishment, drawing a steady stream of customers. I visited for a weekday lunch, so there was no wait. The presence of weekend queue guides at the entrance suggests it gets busy on Saturdays and Sundays. The dining room was not crowded during our visit.

​

The menu is simple, offering only two main choices: cold wheat noodles (*Mul-milmyeon*) and spicy mixed wheat noodles (*Bibim-milmyeon*), with steamed dumplings (*Mandu*) as a side.

​

Adding double noodles (*Gop-baegi*) costs 1,000 KRW, while ordering a separate noodle block (*Sari*) is 3,000 KRW. For larger portions, ordering the double option directly is recommended.

​

The broth is prepared using beef and chicken feet, which is a unique base, and aged for over a year. This aging process raised my expectations for the flavor.

The Milmyeon presented a unique appearance. The cold broth is dark and concentrated. The toppings include half a hard-boiled egg, shredded chicken breast, cucumber, and a dollop of *Dadaegi* (spicy chili paste). You can opt to exclude the chili paste when ordering, but the paste itself is mild, meaning most diners can enjoy it as served.

​

​

The cold broth tasted good on its own, but adding a splash of vinegar improved the profile. The broth has a deep, savory umami flavor; without the menu explanation, I would have assumed it was prepared solely with chicken meat and beef.

​

The noodle texture is distinct. It resembles the thickness of Jjolmyeon (chewy wheat noodles) but is pulled slightly thinner. It has a similar bite to Jjolmyeon but is thinner and slightly less elastic. While Jjolmyeon and Naengmyeon (buckwheat cold noodles) feature distinct textures, Milmyeon falls in between, leaning closer to the Jjolmyeon style.

​

The Bibim-milmyeon (spicy mixed noodles) also offered a good experience. It differs from standard Jjolmyeon, featuring a balanced seasoning paste that works well. The sauce would also pair well with standard Jjolmyeon noodles.

​

The price point is higher than typical Jjolmyeon, but the taste was satisfying.

---

​

I rate this restaurant **3.9 out of 5 stars**. Personally, I favor Hamhung-style Naengmyeon. While this did not reach the level of a high-quality Hamhung Naengmyeon, it represents a satisfying preparation of Busan-style Milmyeon. The broth is distinctive, and the flavor profile should suit general preferences. While not a venue that requires a dedicated trip, it remains a solid local dining choice.

​

Note that this evaluation is not a comparison against other Milmyeon establishments, but rather a standalone rating of the dining experience at this restaurant.
