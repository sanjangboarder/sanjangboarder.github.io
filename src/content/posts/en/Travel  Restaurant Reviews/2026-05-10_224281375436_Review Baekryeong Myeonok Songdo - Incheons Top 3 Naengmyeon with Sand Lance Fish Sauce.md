Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

Lately, I have been visiting Michelin Guide restaurants and enjoying their dishes, though they are primarily located in Seoul and Busan. Finding few Michelin-rated establishments in my home city of Incheon, I searched for alternative local favorites using keywords like "Incheon's Top 3 Jjamppong" and "Incheon's Top 3 Naengmyeon." This search led me to a restaurant named **Baekryeong Myeonok**.

​

While their main branch is located in Jemuilpo rather than Songdo, the main shop was closed for its regular Tuesday holiday. Searching for alternative branches, I discovered they operate a location in Songdo. It is located on the second floor of the Canal Walk Winter Pavilion. Although I walk past this area regularly, I was completely unaware of it. You can park in the Winter Pavilion lot, and they provide a generous 3 hours of complimentary parking when you leave.

​

​

Songdo's Canal Walk has seen declining commercial activity over the years. Having been open for over 10 years, the outdoor mall feels aged and is due for a renovation. While it originally featured a diverse mix of retail stores, it has transitioned primarily into a dining hub. Since the underperforming shops have closed, mostly solid restaurants remain, making it a decent dining destination.

​

​

While the entrance on the second floor is narrow, the interior of Baekryeong Myeonok is spacious with a large number of tables. It seems many locals visit here. I previously reviewed the Sokcho Kodari Naengmyeon restaurant in Canal Walk, which I find average and visit only occasionally. Now, I have a solid alternative.

​

​

The menu focuses on cold noodles. The noodle style aligns with Pyongyang-style naengmyeon (milder, buckwheat-based). Given a choice between Hamheung-style (chewy starch noodles) and Pyongyang-style, I always prefer Hamheung-style. However, their Pyongyang-style noodles are designed to be eaten with sand lance fish sauce (kkana-ri), which immediately piqued my curiosity.

​

In addition to the standard Mul (water) and Bibim (spicy) naengmyeon, they offer "Ban (Half-and-Half) Naengmyeon." Ban Naengmyeon is a middle ground between the two.

​

I typically order spicy bibim naengmyeon and pour cold broth over it. The Ban Naengmyeon arrives pre-set as bibim noodles swimming in cold broth.

​

To complement the noodles, we also ordered bossam (boiled pork slices) and wang-mandu (large dumplings).

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfMjYx/MDAxNzc3OTYyNzI3NzU4.5pzzjTlo4JP_LNnHW8N3dAMzBucZ-bKtOHydWLpEt6Ig.yaSyy3XanuYflRc0HTvzSjzX7L-t_WFaMsywfwyLsI4g.JPEG/SE-afe5aeea-4843-11f1-b572-217d84e1ea4d.jpg?type=w800)

​

On the table is sand lance fish sauce (kkana-ri), an uncommon condiment at naengmyeon restaurants. I was eager to see how it affected the broth.

​

I expected the kettle to hold hot seasoned broth (yuk-su), but it contained plain hot noodle-cooking water (myeon-su). They serve buckwheat noodles, so it tasted like buckwheat tea. As a lover of seasoned hot yuk-su, I found this slightly disappointing.

​

​

The side dishes for the bossam pork include salted shrimp, seasoned soy sauce, and pickled garlic.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfMjc3/MDAxNzc3OTU5MTA3ODA2.IjaZzLvhC6giNcg6StmKVNNP1JG1XcKH0X_RbM_y-QUg.ak2sRBgoy8nsUG1p7Iw6rX0puKHEWL2jCiVwfwU5Jfsg.JPEG/900_20260505_133818.jpg?type=w800)

​

The bossam kimchi is a aged, sour style (mukeun-ji) that is clearly house-made and tasted excellent.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfMTE2/MDAxNzc3OTU5MTExMjMz.9sLev_ARql29paJ2y2PJ8EGLOQuYk8jI1HF-wji5cnsg.D08rEm31vDHzx3Qbxx_3P1kcHajDyYAUhiCo4rhamHkg.JPEG/900_20260505_133819.jpg?type=w800)

​

However, the standard pickled radish and altari radish served with the noodles tasted overly raw and were slightly disappointing.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfMjkx/MDAxNzc3OTU5MTEwMzU0.8i3I8wHmJ4ew-ma-NImG9kx0qoWct8D-PzepmpSKd4kg.5AkgUlychSgUYnRyLHk6lI7OylTJLA8-QY6UOC9LTXgg.JPEG/900_20260505_133826.jpg?type=w800)

​

The bossam is prepared using pork belly and was boiled until exceptionally tender, almost falling apart in texture.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfOTgg/MDAxNzc7OTU5MTEwMTg2.3gXIV2OHoFc17V3VOXk11N-E1nBSIt_8QtoSIt7dtcQg.HZA37oa3CJhPFIqvN0sRMGTAv3eVUuj7wFYLTnJ4lv8g.JPEG/900_20260505_134431.jpg?type=w800)

​

Pork belly delivers an ideal fat-to-meat ratio, making it hard to get wrong. The meat carried no gamey odor and was very tender, representing a solid bossam.

​

​

Wrapping the bossam pork in the aged kimchi tasted excellent.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfMjUy/MDAxNzc3OTYzMTU0NzY1.TN5_zJUiJOVqdbuWirAXnrId2MPgWxBBd3QfJmWy-f0g.DnRVcBqjQ1hAqNlWmX-aYrgqOwlV2zgvcy6dFDriJT8g.JPEG/SE-aff391a3-4843-11f1-b572-6dd4f3b596c4.jpg?type=w800)

​

The wang-mandu did not taste house-made, appearing instead to be a commercial product. They tasted average and carry a familiar flavor that needs little explanation.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfODEg/MDAxNzc3OTYzMTc5OTY5.5JQUqtR_hOtyVjg83gnFecWu0k0P7LpnbZvQWmRt_n0g.NpNOnkIQNXk5Nzm1MQAhMQKk6K-AcAHndy419mfMqpog.JPEG/SE-aff4ca24-4843-11f1-b572-5771c8fd289d.jpg?type=w800)

​

Next is the Ban Naengmyeon. It is served with a generous dollop of bibim sauce sitting in cold broth.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfMjk4/MDAxNzc7OTU5MTA2OTI3.zmz7PGHSK3J-Dn4e-i3xEG9DBEE9EV6n8mPHNk6rusEg.dp6V0hrKGLp-NSITo-AhmgGP9_hJ-mb_8vwNoIVePjYg.JPEG/900_20260505_134732.jpg?type=w800)

​

Garnishes include a slice of beef, half a boiled egg, and two slices of cucumber. The portion size is generous.

​

The noodles are medium-width buckwheat noodles, rather than the thin, highly elastic starch noodles found in Hamheung naengmyeon. They break easily when bitten and do not present a highly unique texture.

​

![](https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA1MDVfMjYy/MDAxNzc3OTYzMjc3MjE0.iq6ZCsCSZepCxv7w7ZzHrsu1xwI8HWnShL-rvQs6R38g.lNfEUJLWOulRTb4Ak0sWDDdgNFXIA_Yab8CQPnCgY_Mg.JPEG/SE-b0051dd7-4843-11f1-b572-4b1eef5e251f.jpg?type=w800)

​

The broth was what interested me most. It differed from standard Pyongyang-style broths. Built on a beef base, it is savory rather than bland, tasting closer to the broth used in Hamheung-style mul-naengmyeon. It is accessible for first-timers. Even without adding vinegar or mustard, the broth carries a pleasant, slightly sweet-and-sour profile.

​

Next, I added a splash of the sand lance fish sauce and vinegar.

​

The combination triggers an explosion of umami. The acidity of the vinegar completely masks any fishy notes from the sauce, creating a rich, deeply savory profile. I immediately understood why people rave about this combination. Adding a moderate amount of sand lance fish sauce and vinegar is absolutely essential here.

​

Applying my subjective criteria, I rate this restaurant **3.9 out of 5 stars**. It represents a solid destination to visit when craving cold noodles. While I would have preferred the chewy texture of Hamheung-style noodles, the rich umami broth created by adding the sand lance fish sauce was a unique, highly satisfying experience.

​

With the summer heat approaching, this serves as an excellent naengmyeon destination in Songdo.

​

Thank you.
