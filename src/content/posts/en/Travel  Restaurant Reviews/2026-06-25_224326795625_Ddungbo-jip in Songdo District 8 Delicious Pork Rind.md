Too lazy to cook at home, I visited a restaurant nearby with my kids that I'd always been curious about. It's called Ttungbozip, a place that sells meat. It's located in the commercial complex of Hoban Summit in Songdo 8-gonggu.
<div class="map-card glass"><div class="map-info"><span class="map-icon">📍</span><div class="map-text"><div class="map-name">Ttungbozip Songdo Branch</div><div class="map-addr">167, 1st Floor, Building 111, Landmark-ro 20, Yeonsu-gu, Incheon</div></div></div><a href="https://map.naver.com/p/search/뚱보집+송도점" target="\_blank" class="map-btn">View Map</a></div>

As you can see from the sign, it's a pork specialty restaurant. While mapping out its location for this post, I realized it's a chain. The signboard in front of Ttungbozip mentioned "special cuts," which piqued my interest as it seemed to focus more on those rather than just samgyeopsal (pork belly) or moksal (pork neck).

Perhaps because I visited on a weekday evening, the restaurant interior was quite spacious. From what I've seen passing by, it doesn't seem to be a place that's usually packed with long queues.

Ttungbozip uses Seonjin Pork? They claim to use only Grade 1 female pigs. If you look at the menu, you'll see quite a few special cuts: mosorisal, gaorisal, kkodeulsal, and pig skin (kkaepdaegi). They also have ogyeopsal (five-layered pork belly) and moksal (pork neck). The price is around 16,000 won for a 160g serving, which I'd say is more of an average price range rather than particularly good value for money.

This is the basic side dish setup. It's not the kind of place that goes all out on banchan; it seems more like they're putting their focus on the quality and taste of the meat itself.

Three types of sauces are served, along with pajeori (spicy green onion salad), which has a unique touch: a raw egg yolk on top.

As for myeong-i namul (marinated wild garlic leaves), kimchi, and pickled radish, there's nothing particularly special; you can think of it as a standard set of commercially available side dishes.

Myeoljeot (fermented anchovy sauce) is served to be boiled with Cheongyang chili peppers and sliced garlic, eaten with the meat... This combination is always a winner!

This is what the large assorted meat platter looks like when ordered. I can see ogyeopsal (five-layered pork belly), samgyeopsal (pork belly), and hangjeongsal (pork jowl), and the rest appear to be special cuts. The platter mainly consists of cuts with a good fat distribution for flavor, rather than lean meat. Personally, these are all cuts I enjoy.

At Ttungbozip, the charcoal grill uses "beonggaetan" (briquette charcoal) style fuel. The heat was decent, but it was a bit disappointing that it wasn't traditional charcoal. Overall, the large assorted platter had good meat quality and tasted great. While I don't drink, I imagine it would be quite satisfying paired with a shot of soju.

Eating just meat felt a bit plain, so we ordered gyeran-jjim (steamed egg). It's not the "volcanic" style that puffs up a lot, but just an average gyeran-jjim.

Personally, I enjoy cuts with a chewy texture and a good balance of fat, so I was generally pleased with the selection of meat. Rather than one particular cut being exceptionally delicious, it was fun to pick and choose from the variety.

It's a bit disappointing that they don't have naengmyeon (cold noodles), which is a staple at most meat restaurants, but they do have bibim-myeon (spicy mixed noodles), so I ordered that.
Eating bibim-myeon with grilled meat is practically cheating because it's so good! I'd recommend this combination over plain rice. I actually wanted to try the doenjang jjageuli (soybean paste stew), but it was sold out.

Feeling that the amount of meat wasn't quite enough, I additionally ordered kkaepdaegi (pig skin) and moksal (pork neck). The moksal felt similar to other lean cuts, but the kkaepdaegi here had a unique characteristic. Unlike other cuts, it's served pre-grilled, and what's different from the pig skin I've had before is that it's quite thick.

While the moksal wasn't particularly memorable, the pre-grilled kkaepdaegi was easy to cook and cooked quickly, which was convenient. Its thickness also gave it a somewhat soft and tender texture, I'd say.

I was a bit surprised that soybean powder came out as a basic condiment, but then I wondered if it was because I ordered pig skin. The price of the kkaepdaegi is a bit higher compared to other meat restaurants, but the taste was good. It made me think that next time I visit, I should order more pig skin than other cuts of meat.

<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjA2MTVfMTgy/MDAxNzgxNTA0MTk2MTQw.s7J2hlbxdxzTa7nS-8oTBux9dEsCT4UoI9eeQj_fmIgg.jumB6_VdpIk8xuz-gP91fr0HFCx_CPDV_Wycgpgkee0g.JPEG/900_20260613_205301.jpg?type=w800" />

If I were to rate Ttungbozip in Songdo 8-gonggu, Incheon, I'd give it 3.8 out of 5 stars. While it's hard to recommend it as a restaurant worth traveling a long distance for, if you enjoy pig skin, I don't think you'd regret coming to try it.
Since it's close to my home, I'll likely stop by whenever I'm craving pig skin. I also think it would be even better if they offered a wider variety of side dishes. For those curious, please keep this in mind.

Thank you.
