Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

During the Lunar New Year holiday, I traveled to Busan. It has been a long time since my last visit, and my concept for this trip was to seek out highly-rated dining spots. I selected candidates and visited them based on proximity.

​

The first destination was **Hapcheon Gukbap-jib** in Yongho-dong, Busan. It is widely recognized as a top-tier pork soup destination. I realized upon arrival that it operates as a popular neighborhood establishment.

​

​

Roadside parking is permitted near the restaurant, though spaces are limited. The dedicated parking lot is located downhill, opposite the police box, but it only accommodates approximately six vehicles.

​

After parking, I approached the restaurant. There were two separate storefronts with identical signage. A passerby clarified that they are the same business. Fortunately, there was no waiting line when I arrived, so I was able to enter immediately.

​

The interior has the feel of a historic eatery (*No-po*). I arrived at approximately 12:00 PM on the final day of the holiday. While there was no queue, the dining room was full of customers.

​

Near the entrance, the staff boil, slice, and ladle the pork soup continuously. The busy pace of ordering and service indicates the high volume of customers they manage. Shortly after I sat down, a queue formed outside.

​

The menu is simple. The *Ttaro-gukbap* contains pork meat served with rice in a separate bowl. The mixed version (*Seokkeo-gukbap*) contains equal portions of meat and intestines. Other variations include intestines-only options or combinations with *Soondae* (blood sausage). In Busan, a *Suyuk Baekban* refers to a pork soup set where the boiled pork slices are served on a separate plate rather than in the broth. We ordered two *Ttaro-gukbap* and two mixed bowls.

The side dishes include *Jeongguji* (seasoned chives), soy dipping sauce, ssamjang, salted shrimp, green chilies, garlic, and onions.

​

For pork soup restaurants, the quality of the *Seokbakji* (sliced radish kimchi) and cabbage kimchi is a key factor. The *Seokbakji* here is unique; it is prepared with sea pineapple (*Meongge*), which provides a clean, refreshing flavor profile that I enjoyed.

​

The cabbage kimchi is prepared in-house. It is fresh and has a satisfying flavor profile. A pork soup restaurant with this standard of kimchi and radish slices represents a successful establishment.

First is the *Ttaro-gukbap*. It contains pork meat, and the broth is clear rather than milky. A small amount of *Dadaegi* (spicy seasoning paste) is included.

​

​

The broth is clean and lacks any gamy pork odor. This clear style contrast with thick, milky versions, offering a flavor profile that should suit most diners. If you prefer a clean broth, this style of Busan pork soup is a good option.

The meat quality is a key factor in pork soup. The pork slices are clean and well-prepared, functioning successfully as *Suyuk* (boiled pork slices). The cut appeared to be front shoulder (*Ap-dari-sal*), featuring a balanced ratio of meat and fat.

​

The offal portion, likely pork tripe, was clean and lacked any unpleasant odor.

​

I tasted the broth first before mixing in the *Dadaegi* paste; both styles were successful. The seasoning paste is mild, making it approachable.

​

<div class="single-image">
<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjAyMTlfODkg/MDAxNzcxNDYwNTUzMTg5.Q8SswmFQKJ1BYn0Uph2klv0-jXoCNekfNJ2Il1Q-CWwg.mZGp2VffbGKcqoGDSyyHL9wt5or_zBco9HKJV3DEXx0g.JPEG/900%EF%BC%BF20260218%EF%BC%BF121850.jpg?type=w800" alt="Mixing dadaegi red paste" />

​

Local custom in Busan is to add seasoned chives (*Jeongguji*) directly into the soup. I tried this method, but did not note a substantial difference in flavor compared to eating the soup plain and pairing the chives separately with the meat.

​

​

Although Busan pork soup is famous, I was skeptical about how different it could be. However, my visit to Hapcheon Gukbap-jib left a highly positive impression.

​

The broth was clean, and the meat and offal cuts were satisfying. It is worth visiting if you are traveling to Busan.

​

The location is not easily accessible via public transit, being situated away from central Busan and primary tourist areas.

​

However, for diners who prioritize flavor, it is worth the trip.

​

I rate this restaurant **4.8 out of 5 stars**. It is highly recommended, and I would return on a future trip to Busan.
