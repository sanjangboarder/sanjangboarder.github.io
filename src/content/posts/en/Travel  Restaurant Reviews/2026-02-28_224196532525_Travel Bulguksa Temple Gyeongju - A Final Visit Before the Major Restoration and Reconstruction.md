Hello, this is SanjangBorder.

​

During my recent trip to Busan and Gyeongju, one of my primary destinations was the historic temples: **Bulguksa and Seokguram Grotto**. Seokguram Grotto is located near the summit of Mt. Toham, so we visited there first before driving down to Bulguksa. Because the descent represents approximately 90% of the drive, the trip computer displayed a regenerative fuel economy of 207 km/L (99+ MPG equivalent).

​

​

Bulguksa Temple is situated at the foot of Mt. Toham, below Seokguram. A walking trail also connects the two sites.

​

We visited on a Friday, yet the grounds were busy with tourists. Given its status as a UNESCO World Heritage site, Bulguksa remains the primary landmark associated with Gyeongju.

​

<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjAyMjRfMjMg/MDAxNzcxOTI3MTg0Nzc8.JhtPa-1fDTj8P5W0I0R7TrCSjmm88AxmLBnXixrudqog.VNIfXDI2HkRa9wmWX68epx6JolkoEB2imwOD5mo7loIg.JPEG/900%EF%BC%BF20260220%EF%BC%BF123931.jpg?type=w800" alt="Main entrance gate at Bulguksa" />

​

After my visit, I read a news report indicating that Bulguksa is scheduled for a **complete dismantling and restoration**. Due to structural aging, a decision was made to disassemble the wood structures beginning in the second half of 2026, followed by a 2-to-3-year restoration project. This means visitors have approximately six months remaining to view the temple in its current state.

​

<img src="https://mblogthumb-phinf.pstatic.net/MjAyNjAyMjRfMjQ3/MDAxNzcxOTI3MTg4Mzg5.CFXeoXQWTeQTBRNF8wA6C0pRIdjc9yMhWoi3BjUNk28g.ydGxbF6uj3ODelQnjT2jCsfBPFv_OTKucbAWVUsTdtQg.JPEG/900%EF%BC%BF20260220%EF%BC%BF123936.jpg?type=w800" alt="Stone monuments outside the gate" />
</div>

​

According to the temple guide map, Bulguksa was established by Prime Minister Kim Dae-seong during the Silla Dynasty, who also oversaw the construction of Seokguram Grotto. The wooden structures were destroyed by fire during the Imjin War (the Japanese invasions of 1592) but were reconstructed in 1973 through a major national restoration project, establishing the current layout.

This is Museoljeon, a hall traditionally used for lecturing and studying Buddhist scriptures. The aging of the structures is apparent throughout the grounds, particularly in the woodwork of Daeungjeon and Museoljeon. Because these wooden buildings are susceptible to weathering, the scheduled restoration is necessary. I plan to visit again once the reconstruction is complete.
